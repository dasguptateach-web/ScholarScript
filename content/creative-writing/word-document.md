## ANSWER KEY

## Detailed explanation for selected difficult questions:

Q21: Milton, Paradise Lost IV.75: “Which way I fly is Hell; myself am Hell.” Scansion: “Which way” (˘ ´) iamb, “I fly” (˘ ´) iamb, “is Hell” (´ ´) spondee, “myself” (˘ ´) iamb, “am Hell” (´ ´) spondee? But careful – “myself am Hell” – “myself” iamb, “am” unstressed? Actually reading aloud: my SELF am HELL – “am” is weaker, but could be promoted. The standard scansion gives a spondee in the third foot (“is Hell”) and a possible spondee in the fifth. Option (C) correctly identifies iambic baseline with spondaic substitution in third foot.

Q25: Pope’s line – the pyrrhic+spondee (ends the song) creates a heavy, conclusive weight, as explained.

Q33: Gray’s “Elegy” line: “The lowing herd wind slowly o’er the lea” – the third foot “herd wind” is a spondee (both stressed), which slows the line to mimic the slow movement of the herd. Hence answer (A).

Q48: Milton’s Paradise Lost is famous for its frequent trochaic inversions and spondaic substitutions, used to create solemnity and emphasis on theological concepts. Pope’s heroic couplets are much more regular; Wordsworth’s Lyrical Ballads use varied metres but not predominantly this profile; Tennyson’s In Memoriam uses iambic tetrameter with occasional variations. So (B) is correct.

Q50: Enjambment that undermines the closure of the ABAB rhyme scheme is most typical of the Shakespearean sonnet quatrains, where the sense often runs across the quatrain boundary, despite the rhyme giving a false sense of pause. Heroic couplets tend to be endstopped; rhyme royal and ottava rima have different structural logics. Thus (A) is correct.
