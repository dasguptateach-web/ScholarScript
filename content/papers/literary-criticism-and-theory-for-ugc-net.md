## Historical Positioning

Plato is the first major philosopher of art in the Western tradition. His critique of poetry in The Republic (especially Book X) sets the terms for every subsequent defence of literature — including Aristotle's response, Sidney's Apology, and Shelley's Defence of Poetry. Virtually every "defence of poetry" written after Plato is, in some sense, a response to

**Plato's accusations.**

## Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Theory of Forms/Ideas (Mimesis)

For Plato, ultimate reality consists of eternal, perfect Forms (e.g., the Form of Beauty, the Form of Justice). The physical world is an imperfect copy of these Forms. Art is a copy of a copy — therefore, it is three steps removed from truth.  Example: A carpenter makes a bed (copy of the Form of Bed). A painter paints the carpenter's bed. The painting is an imitation (mimesis) of an imitation. Poetry is similarly twice removed from truth. 2. Poetic Inspiration vs. Knowledge Plato argues that poets do not write from knowledge or skill (techne) but from divine inspiration or madness (mania). Their work is therefore unreliable as a guide to truth. For the examination, note the contrast: Plato distrusts inspiration; later Romantic critics (Wordsworth, Shelley) celebrate it. 3. The Moral Critique of Poetry (Book X) Poetry appeals to the lower parts of the soul (emotion, appetite) rather than reason. By arousing pity, fear, and laughter, it weakens our rational self-control. Plato therefore banishes poets from the ideal state — with one exception: hymns to the gods and praises of virtuous men. 4. Irony and Performance Plato makes his arguments about the untruth of art through highly crafted literary dialogues — a paradox that students should note. He uses allegories and extended metaphors (the Cave, the Divided Line, the Ship of State) even as he condemns figurative language.

## Historical Positioning

Aristotle was a student of Plato but fundamentally disagreed with his teacher about the value of art. While Plato saw poetry as a degraded copy of a copy, Aristotle saw mimesis as natural and educational. For Aristotle, poetry is more philosophical than history, because it deals with universals, not particulars. His Poetics is the foundational text of Western literary theory, and his concepts of catharsis, hamartia, peripeteia, and anagnorisis remain essential to the study of drama and narrative.

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Mimesis as Natural and Valuable

Contra Plato, Aristotle argues that mimesis is innate to humans — we learn through imitation, and we take pleasure in recognising imitations. Poetry is not a copy of a copy but a representation of universal human action. For Aristotle, mimesis is not degraded but essential. 3 DASGUPTA

The Six Elements of Tragedy (in descending order of importance) Rank Element (Greek) English Term What It Means 1 Mythos Plot The arrangement of incidents — the "soul of tragedy" 2 Ethos Character The moral qualities of the agents 3 Dianoia Thought The intellectual content — what characters say and argue 4 Lexis Diction The verbal expression and choice of words 5 Melos Song / Melody The musical elements of the drama 6 Opsis Spectacle Visual elements (least important for Aristotle) 3. Hamartia (Tragic Flaw) Not merely a "moral flaw" in the Christian sense. Hamartia is better understood as an error of judgement or a mistake that leads to the protagonist's downfall. Oedipus kills his father not out of wickedness but out of ignorance — this is hamartia. 4. Peripeteia (Reversal of Fortune) A sudden reversal in the action, often from good fortune to bad. In Oedipus, the messenger arrives to relieve Oedipus's fear of marrying his mother — but the message has the opposite effect, revealing the truth. That is peripeteia. 5. Anagnorisis (Recognition) A change from ignorance to knowledge. The protagonist realises the truth about himself, his family, or his situation. The most powerful tragedies combine anagnorisis with peripeteia, as in Oedipus. 6. Catharsis (Emotional Purification) The purgation of pity and fear through the experience of tragedy. The audience does not simply feel emotion; they feel it aesthetically and are cleansed of excess emotion. This is one of the most debated concepts in criticism.

Peri Hypsous ("On the Sublime") was for centuries attributed to the 3rd-century critic Cassius Longinus, but the author is now unknown — hence "Pseudo-Longinus." ## Core Concepts (Exam-Focused)

The Five Sources of Sublimity (the ultimate exam target)

Source English Term What It Means 1 Grandeur of thought The capacity to conceive great ideas 2 Powerful emotion (pathos) Genuine, inspired feeling 3 Appropriate use of figures Rhetorical devices (schemes and tropes) deployed effectively 4 Noble diction Choice of words, metaphors, and verbal arrangements 5 Dignified and elevated composition Structure, arrangement, and the "music" of language The common ground of all five — the preliminary gift on which all five sources depend — is command of language. ## Historical Positioning

Horace's Epistle to the Pisos (commonly called Ars Poetica) became the most influential practical treatise on poetry during the Renaissance and Neoclassical periods. Where Aristotle gave rules for writing tragedy, Horace gave advice for the poet — on unity, decorum, opening lines, characterisation, the need for labour and revision, and the balance of pleasure and instruction. Horace is the source of countless maxims that appear on the UGC NET.

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Ut Pictura Poesis ("As is painting, so is poetry") The most famous phrase from Horace. It draws an analogy between painting and poetry — both are imitative arts, both can be understood and judged. Important nuance: Horace meant that a poem, like a painting, requires the viewer/reader to stand at the appropriate distance; later critics (especially the 18th century) used the phrase to argue that poetry should be vividly pictorial. 2. Decorum (The principle of appropriateness) Style, language, and behaviour must be appropriate to character, situation, and genre. A king should not speak like a slave; comedy should not use tragic diction; a young poet should not boast of age and wisdom. 3. The Poet's Dual Role: "Omne tulit punctum qui miscuit utile dulci" Translation: "He has won every vote who has mixed the useful with the pleasant." Poetry should both instruct (utile) and delight (dulce). This principle dominated Renaissance and Neoclassical criticism. 4. Labour and Revision (Ars est celare artem — "the art is to conceal art") The best poetry seems effortless and natural, but it is the result of careful, laborious revision. Horace advises the poet to keep a work for nine years before publishing — a counsel of perfection that few followed. 5. "Bonus dormitat Homerus" ("Even good Homer nods") Even the greatest poets sometimes produce inferior work — a crucial point for the critic who might otherwise worship authority uncritically.

Key Works  Ars Poetica (Epistle to the Pisos), c. 19 BCE

## Historical Positioning

Sidney's Apology is the first major work of English literary criticism and the most important Renaissance defence of poetry. It is a direct response to the attacks on poetry that had been circulating since Plato, and to Puritan objections that poetry was frivolous, lying, and immoral. Sidney synthesises classical, Italian, and English critical traditions into a powerful synthesis that remained influential for two centuries.

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Poetry as the "First Light-Giver to Ignorance" Sidney argues that poetry is the oldest and most ancient of all arts. All other disciplines (philosophy, history, law) borrow from poetry. The poet is not a liar but a maker (from the Greek poiesis, "making"). 2. The Poet's Superiority to the Philosopher and the Historian  The philosopher teaches virtue but abstractly; he gives "precept without example." His lessons are cold and difficult to apply.  The historian gives examples but without moral precept; he shows what has happened, not what should happen.  The poet does both: he gives precept through example, showing virtue as attractive and vice as repulsive. He is, therefore, the most effective teacher of virtue. 3. The Poet as Maker The poet is not bound by nature; he can create golden worlds superior to the "brazen" world of nature. This is not lying — it is creation. The poet's world is a better, more ordered world than the fallen world of everyday experience. 8 DASGUPTA

Sidney systematically rebuts four charges:  Poetry is a waste of time. Reply: What better use of time than learning virtue through pleasure?  Poetry is the mother of lies. Reply: The poet never affirms anything as true; he "nothing affirms, and therefore never lieth."  Poetry infects the soul with evil desires. Reply: Poetry does not cause vice; vice lies in the reader, not in the text.

Blame the abuse, not the art.  Plato banished poets. Reply: Plato did not banish poetry itself but the abuse of it. And Plato's own dialogues are themselves highly poetic.

## Historical Positioning

Dryden was England's first professional literary critic. He dominated English letters after the Restoration (1660), and his essays — on dramatic poesy, on satire, on translation, on the epic — established the vocabulary and standards of English Neoclassicism. He is the crucial link between Renaissance criticism (Sidney, Jonson) and the full-blown

**Augustanism of Pope and Johnson.**

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. An Essay of Dramatic Poesy (1668)

The masterpiece of Dryden's criticism. Framed as a dialogue among four speakers representing different positions:  Crites (Sir Robert Howard): Defends the ancients (especially the Three Unities)  Eugenius (Charles Sackville): Defends the moderns (English drama, which violates the unities)  Lisideius (Sir Charles Sedley): Defends French drama (especially the unities)  Neander (Dryden himself): Occupies a middle position — respects the ancients but argues for English drama's superiority in vigour of characterisation and variety of action 2. The Doctrine of Three Unities (Neoclassical orthodoxy)

Derived from misinterpretation of Aristotle:  Unity of Action: One main plot; no sub-plots (Aristotle did require this)  Unity of Time: The action should occur within 24 hours (Aristotle noted this as typical practice, not a rule)  Unity of Place: The action should occur in one location (not in Aristotle at all — a Renaissance addition) Dryden defends Shakespeare against the charge of violating the unities by arguing that variety and richness compensate for formal looseness. 3. Dryden on Shakespeare, Jonson, and Fletcher Dryden's comparative judgements are exam-favourite material: 10 DASGUPTA

"He was the man who of all modern, and perhaps ancient poets, had the largest and most comprehensive soul."  Ben Jonson: The most learned and correct (adhering to rules) but lacked the "wit" and "fancy" of Shakespeare.  Fletcher: Excelled in representation of love but was "somewhat languishing." 4. On Satire and Translation Dryden's essays on satire (especially the Discourse on Satire) and his prefaces to his translations of Virgil, Ovid, and Juvenal defined the Neoclassical theory of translation: "paraphrase" (faithful to sense but free in expression) rather than "metaphrase" (literal word-for-word) or "imitation" (loose adaptation).

## Historical Positioning

Samuel Johnson is the dominant figure of 18th-century English criticism. His Lives of the Poets (1779–81) established the biographical method of criticism — reading a poet's work in the context of their life and times. His Preface to Shakespeare (1765) is the most important single document in the reception history of Shakespeare.

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Preface to Shakespeare (1765) – Johnson's Major Contributions

## Defence of the Unities (Partially):

Johnson famously defends Shakespeare against the charge of violating the unities of time and place. His argument: The audience knows the stage is a stage and the actors are actors. If they can accept that the stage represents Egypt, they can accept that three hours of stage time represent years of action. Only unity of action is essential.

## Judgement on Shakespeare's Faults:

Johnson does not worship Shakespeare uncritically. He identifies specific defects:  Puns and quibbles: Shakespeare is addicted to them; they are his "fatal Cleopatra."  Anachronisms: Hector quoting Aristotle in Troilus and Cressida.  Loss of tragic dignity: Shakespeare mixes comedy with tragedy (Johnson prefers the purity of Neoclassical form).  Careless plotting: Especially in the comedies.

## The "Votaries of Antiquity" Passage:

Johnson attacks those who praise ancient works not from reason but from prejudice. This is a key passage in the Preface: "Antiquity, like every other quality that attracts the notice of mankind, has undoubtedly votaries that reverence it, not from reason, but from prejudice." 2. The Lives of the Poets – Biographical Criticism Johnson examines poets (Cowley, Milton, Dryden, Pope, Gray, and others) through the lens of their lives and personalities. This approach remained dominant until the New Criticism of the 20th century (which rejected biography). Johnson's judgement on Milton's Lycidas — "the diction is harsh, the rhymes uncertain, and the numbers unpleasing" — has been endlessly debated. 3. Johnson on Metaphysical Poetry (from Life of Cowley) Johnson coined the term "Metaphysical Poetry" (though Dryden had earlier used "Metaphysical" pejoratively). His famous definition: "The most heterogeneous ideas are yoked by violence together." This is a hostile definition — Johnson preferred the clarity and order of Neoclassical verse. 13 DASGUPTA

## Historical Positioning

Wordsworth's Preface is the manifesto of English Romanticism. It broke decisively with Neoclassical poetics (which had dominated for over a century) and established the new Romantic principles: ordinary life, simple language, spontaneous feeling, and nature as teacher. It is the single most important document in Romantic literary theory. 14 DASGUPTA

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. The Subject Matter of Poetry

Poetry should take its subject matter from "incidents and situations from common life" and treat them in "a selection of language really used by men." This was a direct assault on the "poetic diction" of the 18th century — the specialised vocabulary (e.g., "finny tribe" for fish) that Pope and Gray had cultivated. 2. The Language of Poetry The most controversial claim: There is no essential difference between the language of poetry and the language of prose. Both use the same language when both are "well written." This erased the distinction between "poetic" and "ordinary" language. 3. Poetry as "Spontaneous Overflow of Powerful Feeling" The most famous phrase in the Preface. Poetry originates in emotion recollected in tranquillity — the poet first experiences a powerful emotion, then, in a calm moment, recollects that emotion, and finally writes it down. The process is: emotion → recollection → composition. 4. The Poet as "Man Speaking to Men" The poet is not a privileged being set apart but an ordinary human being with "greater promptness to think and feel" and a "more comprehensive soul." The poet speaks to other humans, not above them. 5. Poetry as the Breath of a "Man of Science" Wordsworth distinguishes the poet from the man of science: the scientist seeks truth as a "remote and unknown benefactor"; the poet sings "in which all human beings join with him, rejoices in the presence of truth as our visible friend and hourly companion."

## Historical Positioning

Coleridge's Biographia Literaria is the most philosophical work of English Romantic criticism. Where Wordsworth wrote a manifesto, Coleridge wrote a theory of imagination grounded in German Idealism (Kant, Schelling). The Biographia is notoriously difficult — part autobiography, part literary theory, part philosophical treatise — but it contains Coleridge's most enduring contributions: the distinction between primary and secondary imagination, and the distinction between imagination and fancy.

## Historical Positioning

Edward Said's Orientalism (1978) is one of the most influential academic books of the 20th century. It founded the field of postcolonial studies and changed how the West studies the "Orient." Said was a Palestinian-American literary theorist and a public intellectual.

## Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. The Subaltern

Spivak borrows the term subaltern from Antonio Gramsci (who used it for the proletariat). HOMI K. BHABHA (1949– )

## Historical Positioning

Bhabha is the theorist of hybridity, mimicry, and the Third Space. His work — especially The Location of Culture (1994) — has been enormously influential in postcolonial studies and cultural studies. ## Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Hybridity

Colonial contact produces hybrid forms — neither fully coloniser nor fully colonised. Hybridity disrupts the binary opposition of coloniser/colonised, revealing that both are mutually contaminated. Hybridity is not a celebration of mixing; it is the disturbing, in-between space that deconstructs colonial authority. 2. The Third Space The space of negotiation between coloniser and colonised — neither one nor the other. The Third Space is where new, hybrid identities emerge. It is the space of cultural translation, not cultural origin. 3. Colonial Mimicry The colonised subject mimics the coloniser — adopts the coloniser's language, manners, clothes, and customs. But mimicry is never perfect — it is always almost the same but not quite. This "not quite" is threatening to colonial authority: the mimic is a "menacing" figure, both like the coloniser and not like him. 4. Ambivalence Colonial discourse is not stable — it is characterised by ambivalence, the simultaneous attraction and repulsion to the colonial other. The coloniser both desires and fears the colonised. This ambivalence is a site of slippage — where colonial authority can be unsettled. 5. Stereotype as Fetish The colonial stereotype is not a simple misrepresentation. It is a fetish — a substitute for an impossible knowledge. The stereotype fixes the colonial subject in a repetitive, anxious form (the "lazy native," the "lustful black").

## Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. The Implied Reader

The implied reader is not the actual reader (a real person) but the text's ideal reader — the role and activity that the text itself structures. The text implies a reader with certain competencies, attitudes, and expectations. Reading is the actualisation of this role. 2. Gaps (Leerstellen) and Indeterminacy Every literary text contains gaps — places where the text does not say everything, where there are ambiguities, ellipses, open possibilities.  These gaps are not defects — they are the condition of the text's aesthetic power. The reader must fill the gaps — actively constructing meaning.  Indeterminacy: The quality of the text that requires the reader's interpretive activity to become determinate. 3. Wandering Viewpoint (Wandernde Blickpunkt) Reading is not a static act. The reader's viewpoint wanders through the text — moving forward, anticipating, retroactively reinterpreting, holding strands in suspension. The meaning of a text is the outcome of this wandering.

## Historical Positioning

Jauss, a German literary historian, was the other leading figure of the Constance School of reception theory. His essay "Literary History as a Challenge to Literary Theory" (1967) argued for a reception-based literary history.

Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Horizon of Expectations (Erwartungshorizont) The horizon of expectations is the set of cultural, literary, and generic norms that readers bring to a text at the time of its publication. ## Historical Positioning

Stanley Fish is the most radical of the reader-response critics. His work — especially Is There a Text in This Class? ## Core Concepts (Exam-Focused) 1. Affective Stylistics

Fish's early method: analyse the reader's experience of reading the text in time. Instead of asking "What does this sentence mean?", ask "What does this sentence do to the reader?" — how does it affect, shape, and manipulate the reader's consciousness. 2. Interpretive Communities Meaning is not produced by individual readers alone. Readers belong to interpretive communities — groups that share interpretive strategies, assumptions, and values. Meaning is community-based — what counts as a "good reading" is established by the community. 3. "Is There a Text in This Class?" The title essay poses the question: Is there a text independent of interpretation? Fish's answer: No. The "text" is never encountered apart from interpretation. The notion of a text "in itself" is an illusion. 4. The Authority of Interpretive Communities Interpretive communities are authoritative — they determine what counts as a valid reading. The community cannot be appealed against — there is no higher court.
