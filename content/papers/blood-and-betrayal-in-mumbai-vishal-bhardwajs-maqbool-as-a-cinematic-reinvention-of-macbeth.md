Blood and Betrayal in Mumbai: Vishal Bhardwaj’s Maqbool as a Cinematic Reinvention of Macbeth

Introduction Vishal Bhardwaj’s Maqbool (2003) drags the bones of Macbeth out of medieval Scotland and buries them in the sweat soaked, money-drenched underworld of contemporary Mumbai. The film is not a polite translation; it is a visceral reimagining that replaces thanes with gangsters, heathland prophecies with horoscopes whispered by two corrupt policemen, and castle corridors with rain-slicked alleyways. Right from its ominous opening, where a circular symbol is drawn in blood-red powder, Bhardwaj signals that fate and ambition are about to collide. What emerges is both a faithful psychological portrait of a man destroyed by his own vaulting desire and a distinctly Indian noir that pulses with Urdu poetry, Sufi music, and the moral exhaustion of a city that never sleeps. This paper examines how Maqbool translates Shakespeare’s tragedy into a new cultural grammar while preserving its terrifying core.

## Film Details

Maqbool was released in India on 30 January 2004, though it premiered at the 2003 Toronto International Film Festival. Vishal Bhardwaj directed and co-wrote the screenplay with Abbas Tyrewala. The film was produced by Bobby Bedi under the Kaleidoscope Entertainment banner. Its stellar cast includes Irrfan Khan as Maqbool, Tabu as Nimmi, Pankaj Kapur as Abbaji, Naseeruddin Shah and Om Puri as the corrupt inspectors, and Masumeh Makhija as Sameera. The cinematography is by Hemant Chaturvedi, while Bhardwaj himself composed the haunting background score and songs. The dialogue, rich with poetic Urdu and Mumbai street slang, was penned by Bhardwaj and Tyrewala. The film runs 132 minutes and is rated

**UA in India.**

History of the Genre/Story and References Macbeth has always been the playwright’s most portable nightmare. Its skeleton—a brave warrior, a whispering wife, a splintered prophecy, and blood that will not wash off—adapts to almost any political landscape. Before Bhardwaj, cinema and theatre had already found countless ways to re-house the Scottish play. The “Voodoo Macbeth,” directed by a twenty year-old Orson Welles for the Federal Theatre Project in 1936, transplanted the action to a Caribbean island with an all Black cast, linking the supernatural to Haitian rituals and exposing the raw nerve of colonial power. In 1976, Trevor Nunn’s minimalist Royal Shakespeare Company production, later filmed for television, stripped the play to a bare stage, a circle of light, and the faces of Ian McKellen and Judi Dench, proving that claustrophobic intimacy could be as terrifying as any castle. On the small screen, the BBC Television Shakespeare offered Jack Gold’s 1983 version starring Nicol Williamson, a studio-bound production that foregrounded the text’s hallucinatory quality with aggressive close-ups and a muddy, medieval palette. More recently, the BBC’s modern-day film Macbeth (2015) with Justin Kurzel pushed the trauma into a gritty, eleventh-century war zone, emphasizing PTSD and grief. All these interpretations share a crucial understanding: the play lives not in kilts and broadswords but in the unravelling mind of its protagonist. Bhardwaj, aware of this lineage, draws on these theatrical and televisual experiments but makes the boldest leap by sinicizing the narrative through the grammar of Bollywood and the Mumbai mafia, replacing the weird sisters with two policemen who giggle over astrology charts, thereby rooting the supernatural firmly in the corrupt everyday (Trivedi 130).

## Critical Review

Maqbool unfolds in a Bombay that feels both real and mythic. Bhardwaj and cinematographer Hemant Chaturvedi construct a world of perpetual murk: offices are tobacco-stained, bedrooms are suffused with a sickly green, and rain seems to fall incessantly, as if the city itself is trying to wash away its sins. The film’s visual grammar leans heavily on chiaroscuro, recalling film noir but filtered through a distinctly South Asian lens—back-alley deals illuminated by a single naked bulb, blood pooling on monsoon-soaked tarmac. Bhardwaj’s own background as a music composer infuses every frame with sonic tension. The background score is a bed of low drones, sudden percussive strikes, and the eerie voice of a Sufi singer, which acts almost like a Greek chorus commenting on the action. Songs, often the curse of gritty crime dramas, are used sparingly and intelligently; they emerge from the environment—a qawwali at a wedding, a ghazal in a brothel— and never break the film’s hypnosis. The performances form the film’s true backbone. Irrfan Khan’s Maqbool is a study in contained terror. His eyes do most of the work: they widen with dread after the prediction, turn glassy during the murder, and finally go hollow with guilt. He never delivers a “tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow” soliloquy, but a single close-up of his face after Nimmi miscarries carries the same unbearable weight. Tabu, as Nimmi, is a revelation. Her Lady Macbeth is not an overtly wicked manipulator but a weary, sensual woman who has spent years as a gangster’s mistress and sees Maqbool as her last chance at domestic dignity. Her seductions are desperate rather than calculating, and when she incites him to kill, she whispers it into his neck with a mixture of lust and maternal urgency. Pankaj Kapur’s Abbaji is a patriarchal mobster who

genuinely loves Maqbool as a son, which makes his vulnerability almost unbearable; his murder in the sanctity of his home, with a pillow pressed over his face, echoes the killing of Duncan with an intimacy that no stage production can replicate. Naseeruddin Shah and Om Puri, playing the bumbling yet menacing cops who prophesy Maqbool’s rise and fall, bring a grotesque comedy that never undercuts the tension. They function as living embodiments of a corrupt state, a universe where the moral order has already collapsed, making the supernatural entirely redundant. This substitution is arguably the film’s greatest strength, because it grounds the play’s metaphysical questions in a biting social critique: in a world where lawmen deal in superstition, what hope is there for justice? The film is not without weaknesses. The romantic subplot involving Sameera, Abbaji’s daughter, feels undercooked and somewhat obligatory, perhaps a concession to commercial sensibilities. At times, the pacing in the second act sags under the weight of its own atmosphere, and a few scenes of gangland negotiation could have been trimmed without losing the thematic richness. Nevertheless, Maqbool remains a landmark in Shakespearean adaptation because it refuses to treat the source as a museum piece. It extracts the play’s psychological DNA and transplants it into a body that, however foreign in appearance, beats with the same dread.

## Personal Critical Evaluation

As a film critic engaging with Maqbool, I find myself returning again and again to the audacity of its adaptations—the choices Bhardwaj makes in what he keeps, what he discards, and what he invents. The most radical transformation is the removal of the supernatural. In Shakespeare’s play, the witches are ontological intruders; they may be real, they may be fate, or they may be projections of Macbeth’s ambition. Bhardwaj replaces them with two policemen who, under the guise of drawing up a horoscope for Abbaji, predict that Maqbool will soon seize control of the underworld. This switch is not just clever; it is ideologically savage. The prophecy no longer descends from an otherworldly realm—it is cooked up by representatives of the law, who are themselves steeped in corruption. When one of the cops later admits, “It was just a joke,” the bottom falls out of Maqbool’s universe. Fate, it turns out, is just a prank played by bored, powerful men. This single change forces us to reconsider the entire mechanism of tragedy: is Maqbool a victim of destiny or of his own gullibility and ambition, fed by a system that manufactures lies for entertainment? The film refuses a comfortable answer, which feels more unsettling than any midnight cauldron on a blasted heath. Nimmi’s character undergoes a similarly profound shift. Lady Macbeth’s “unsex me here” speech is replaced by a quieter, more devastating form of manipulation. Nimmi never asks the spirits to fill her with cruelty; instead, she weaponizes her own marginalization. When she tells Maqbool, “I’m just the mistress, I have no status,” the line stings because it reveals the social architecture of the mafia household. Her goading stems not from pure ambition but from a survival instinct sharpened by years of being a kept woman. Her eventual descent into guilt is rendered through a miscarriage, a bodily loss that substitutes for the play’s sleepwalking scene. The blood that cannot be washed off becomes, in Maqbool, blood that will not stop flowing from her own womb. It is a chillingly corporeal adaptation that ties guilt to gender in a way the original only hints at. Bhardwaj also takes liberties with the play’s structure. Fleance, the son of Banquo, becomes Guddu, the son of a rival gangster, who escapes Maqbool’s hit and lives to carry the possibility of a different future. Crucially, the film does not end with a new king crowned but with the city carrying on as it always has—ambulances wailing, men lighting cigarettes, the rain still falling. The absence of Malcolm or any restorative figure suggests a cyclical violence that no amount of bloodshed can purge. For an Indian audience familiar with the mythological concept of kalyug (the age of darkness), this resonates on a deeper philosophical register. The film becomes not just a story about one man’s fall but a portrait of an epoch bereft of moral renewal. As a critic, I find the film’s use of Urdu poetry and Bhardwaj’s own lyrics to be its secret weapon. Lines from Mirza Ghalib and Faiz Ahmed Faiz are woven into the dialogue, elevating the gangster-speak to a kind of street-side philosophy. When Maqbool finally accepts his doom, he murmurs, “Darr nahi lagta” (I am not afraid), and the weariness in Irrfan’s voice carries the weight of every tragic hero who has ever walked towards a foretold end. This fusion of high poetry and gutter realism gives Maqbool a texture that no English-language adaptation has ever quite achieved—a feeling that the tragedy is being sung as much as it is being performed.

## Illustrations

Scene 1: The Prediction. In a dimly lit police lock-up, inspectors Pandit and Purohit (Shah and Puri) toy with a horoscope in front of Maqbool and Kaka (Banquo figure). The camera circles slowly, the men’s faces half in shadow, as they casually predict that Maqbool will soon take over the empire. The banality of the setting—a grimy police station—contrasts with the seismic shift in Maqbool’s eyes, setting the entire tragedy in motion. Scene 2: The Murder of Abbaji. Abbaji lies asleep in his bedroom, a red sheet draped over him like a shroud pre-empting its function. Maqbool enters, his breathing the loudest sound in the room. He presses a pillow over Abbaji’s face. The camera

holds on the old man’s twitching feet, an unbearably intimate detail that transforms regicide into patricide. After the act, Maqbool looks at his trembling hands, unable to comprehend what they have done. Scene 3: Nimmi’s Breakdown. In a hospital corridor after her miscarriage, Nimmi sits slumped against a wall. The stark white lighting strips away any trace of glamour. She repeatedly rubs her hands against her bloodied shawl, a gesture borrowed from Lady Macbeth’s sleepwalking but made wholly her own. Tabu’s vacant stare, accompanied by a single droning note on the soundtrack, captures a mind that has finally snapped under the weight of guilt.

## Conclusion

Maqbool does not merely transpose Macbeth to a new location; it listens to the silences in Shakespeare’s text and fills them with the specific music of Mumbai. The film understands that tragedy is not contingent on kings and cauldrons but on the human capacity to believe a lie, betray a father-figure, and drown in the resulting guilt. Bhardwaj’s achievement lies in his refusal to treat the source material as sacred. Every change—the cops as weird sisters, the miscarriage as the locus of madness, the absence of a redemptive ending—serves to interrogate the original rather than simply illustrate it. The result is a work that stands on its own as a piece of cinema even as it deepens our understanding of Shakespeare’s vision. Assessing the film critically, its strengths reside overwhelmingly in the central performances. Irrfan Khan, Tabu, and Pankaj Kapur form a triangle of love, ambition, and betrayal that burns with a quiet intensity rarely seen in crime films. The cinematography, with its obsessive focus on rain, reflections, and the colour of dried blood, creates a visual poem that mirrors the psychological descent of the characters. The music, again, is not an afterthought but the film’s circulatory system, pumping unease through every scene. If one were to suggest improvements, the film’s middle stretch could benefit from tighter editing. The romantic subplot involving Sameera could either be excised entirely or developed with more depth to offer a genuine counterpoint to the central relationship. Some of the gangland machinations, while authentic, slow the narrative’s tragic momentum. Yet these are minor complaints against a canvas this ambitious. In a hypothetical director’s cut, Bhardwaj might also restore a few more of the play’s psychological soliloquies, perhaps through voiceover, to let the audience even deeper into Maqbool’s splintering consciousness. But the absence of direct monologue is also a deliberate stylistic choice, one that forces the actors to convey interiority through gesture and glance, and in that challenge they triumph. Watching Maqbool two decades after its release, I am struck by how fresh it still feels. It has not aged because its concerns—the corrupting nature of power, the fragility of loyalty, the way systems of authority manufacture their own fates—are not period-specific. The Mumbai underworld may have changed its face, but the mechanics of ambition it depicts remain brutally relevant. For any student of Shakespeare or cinema, the film offers a masterclass in adaptation: be faithful to the spirit, not the letter; trust your performers to carry the emotional architecture; and never forget that the most profound tragedies are always, at their core, about people who could have been us.
