Addison's "Sir Roger at Home" and Goldsmith's "Parliamentary Elections"

Joseph Addison: ‘Sir Roger at Home’ (The Spectator No. 2, 1711)

## Introduction: The Spectator Project and Its Aims

Joseph Addison’s ‘Sir Roger at Home’ appeared on 2 March 1711 as the second number of The Spectator, the most influential periodical of the eighteenth century. Addison, together with his friend Richard Steele, set out to “enliven morality with wit, and to temper wit with morality,” as Steele declared in the dedication. The paper was aimed at the rising urban middle class—tradesmen, clerks, and their families—who gathered in the new coffee-houses of London. Its purpose was not to report news but to cultivate taste, virtue, and rational conversation. ‘Sir Roger at Home’ is the first full-length portrait of Sir Roger de Coverley, the fictional club member who would become the most celebrated character in the series. Through this essay, Addison created a model of what the periodical essay could achieve: a blend of character sketch, moral reflection, and gentle social satire that educated while it entertained.

Sir Roger de Coverley: The Country Squire as Moral Ideal

Sir Roger is presented not as a hero of action but as a figure of benevolent repose. The Spectator describes him as a gentleman “in the decline of life” who has retired to his country estate, where he exercises a kind of paternal authority over his household and tenants. Addison’s portrait is built up through a series of domestic vignettes: the arrival, the old servants, the chaplain, the picture gallery, and the dinner. The essay abounds with details that render Sir Roger at once comic and deeply sympathetic. One of the most telling moments comes when Sir Roger shows his guest the family portraits, singling out his great-grandfather “who invented the country-dance.” This apparently trivial fact is a masterstroke of characterisation. It reveals Sir Roger’s whimsical pride in the local and the customary rather than in martial or political achievement. It tells us that his world is not the great stage of history but the village green; his values are those of conviviality, continuity, and harmless pleasure.

Sir Roger’s relationship with his servants further deepens the portrait. Addison notes:

“He is the best master in the world to his servants; he seldom dismisses any of them, and his whole family has lived in his house above a dozen years each in their respective stations.”

The word ‘family’ here is crucial. It blurs the boundary between blood relations and hired retainers, suggesting an organic community bound by affection and mutual obligation rather than by contract. This feudal ideal stands in deliberate contrast to the impersonal, money-driven relationships of commercial London. Sir Roger’s household is a miniature commonwealth where loyalty is repaid with security, and the old butler who has served the family for decades is treated with as much respect as a family member. This domestic harmony is not merely sentimental; it is a moral argument. Addison implies that true social order arises from kindness and personal ties, not from abstract rights or economic efficiency.

## Religion and the Chaplain: Piety Without Pomposity

The religious life of Coverley Hall is another vehicle for Addison’s moral vision. Sir Roger maintains a resident chaplain, a “man of good sense and some learning,” who lives “very comfortably” on an annuity provided by his patron. The arrangement is a model of what Addison considered an ideal relationship between the landed gentry and the Church of England: the squire supports a learned clergyman who, in turn, guides the household in devotion and morality. Yet Addison never allows piety to become pompous or self-righteous. He introduces a comic counterpoint through Sir Roger’s candid admission:

“He is a very honest man, and of a very good life, but … he did not much like his preaching, because it is more like a prayer than a sermon.”

And further, the Spectator learns that Sir Roger sometimes falls asleep during the sermon, though he remains a strict observer of church duties. This is humour of the most delicate kind. It does not attack religion; it humanises it. Sir Roger’s simple, sleepy piety is perhaps more genuine, Addison suggests, than the ostentatious devotion of the hypocrite. The critic William Hazlitt captured this blend when he wrote:

“The character of Sir Roger de Coverley is the masterpiece of Addison’s genius, a creation that mixes the laughable and the amiable with inimitable grace.”

Addison makes us smile at the old knight’s foibles, but we never lose respect for his essential goodness. The chaplain, too, is a figure of quiet merit, content with his modest station and valuing peace over preferment. The religious atmosphere of the house is thus one of tolerance, comfort, and unshaken faith—an idealised vision of the rural Anglican parish, far from the theological controversies of the age.

## The Spectator Persona and Narrative Technique

The narrator of the essay, Mr. Spectator, is himself a carefully crafted character. He is not a participant in the action but an observer—urbane, cultivated, and fundamentally sympathetic. His detachment permits him to record Sir Roger’s oddities without malice. When he notes “the joy that appeared in the countenances of these ancient domestics upon my friend’s arrival at his country-seat,” the tone is that of a guest genuinely moved by the scene. This persona acts as a mediating consciousness between the provincial world of Coverley Hall and the sophisticated London reader. He translates rural customs into a language of polite amusement, making them accessible without mocking them. The essay’s structure follows the natural rhythm of a country visit—arrival, tour, meal, departure—creating an effect of casual intimacy that disguises the careful artistry behind it.

C. S. Lewis, in his study of eighteenth-century literature, praised Addison’s prose for its “middle style”—a register that is clear, elegant, and free from both vulgarity and pedantry. The Spectator’s voice embodies this ideal perfectly. He is neither a cynical wit nor a rustic simpleton; he is a gentleman of taste who can appreciate both the sophistication of the town and the wholesome simplicity of the country. Through his eyes, Sir Roger becomes more than a caricature; he becomes a living figure whose weaknesses only endear him more. This narrative technique was revolutionary at the time, bridging the gap between the impersonal essay and the emerging novel. It allowed Addison to combine moral instruction with the pleasure of story-telling, satisfying the dual aim of The Spectator: to delight and to instruct.

## Social Context and Critical Legacy

‘Sir Roger at Home’ must be read against the background of early eighteenth-century England, a period of rapid commercial expansion, political factionalism, and the gradual decline of the old landed order. Sir Roger represents a vanishing ideal of rural paternalism—a world in which the squire knew every tenant by name and felt a personal duty toward his dependents. Addison does not argue for a return to feudalism, but he holds up Sir Roger’s household as a gentle reproof to the greed and anonymity of the new money economy. Samuel Johnson, the greatest critic of the age, later remarked that The Spectator was “the first book to teach the common people to think, and to teach them to think justly.” In ‘Sir Roger at Home,’ this moral education takes the form of an affectionate portrait that makes virtue attractive by making it lovable. The essay remains a cornerstone of the periodical tradition and a classic of English prose, demonstrating that the most effective social criticism often wears the mask of gentle humour.

## Master-Level Study Guide

Oliver Goldsmith: ‘Parliamentary Elections’ (The Citizen of the World, Letter 112, 1762)

Introduction: The Citizen of the World and the Epistolary Tradition

Oliver Goldsmith’s ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is Letter 112 of The Citizen of the World, a collection of fictional letters published in 1762. The letters are presented as the correspondence of Lien Chi Altangi, a Chinese philosopher travelling in England, who reports his observations to his friend Fum Hoam in Peking. The device of the foreign observer was already familiar from Montesquieu’s Persian Letters (1721) and other Enlightenment satires, but Goldsmith invests it with his own distinctive blend of wit, indignation, and moral seriousness. The Chinese persona allows him to defamiliarise English customs—to strip them of their patriotic rhetoric and reveal their underlying absurdity and corruption. ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is one of the sharpest political satires in the collection, offering a scathing portrait of the electoral system that purported to be the foundation of British liberty.

## The Foreign Observer as Satirical Device

The choice of a Chinese narrator is central to the essay’s satirical method. Lien Chi Altangi is not a naive fool; he is a rational and humane philosopher who genuinely expects to find in England the “freedom” of which the English boast. His disappointment is therefore the reader’s indictment. When he arrives at the borough and finds “the whole town one continued scene of riot and confusion,” he is simply reporting what any objective stranger would see. The familiar is made strange: the coloured ribbons, the drunken mobs, the open bribery are presented as the bizarre rituals of a barbarian people. This defamiliarisation is the key satirical technique. It prevents the English reader from taking refuge in jingoistic self-congratulation and forces a confrontation with the ugly realities of electoral practice.

Lien Chi’s cultural distance also gives his judgments a devastating neutrality. He is not a Whig attacking Tories or a Tory attacking Whigs; he is an outsider questioning the very premise of the system. When he remarks that “an election is a kind of a fair, where the principal commodity to be sold is the liberty of the people,” the metaphor is delivered with the calm precision of a natural historian describing an unfamiliar species. This tone—mock-innocence edged with moral outrage—is Goldsmith’s signature. The critic George Saintsbury recognised its importance when he wrote:

“Goldsmith’s Chinese letters are the ancestors of all those works in which a foreign visitor holds up the mirror to European society.”

Goldsmith’s innovation was to combine this philosophical detachment with a warm, humorous sympathy that makes the satire feel less like a lecture and more like a shared joke at the expense of a common folly.

Satirical Targets: Corruption, Venality, and the Myth of Liberty

The primary target of the essay is electoral corruption. Goldsmith depicts a system in which votes are openly bought and sold, candidates are distinguished only by the depth of their purses, and the electorate is a drunken, unprincipled mob. One voter boasts that he has “already sold his vote to both sides, and would vote for neither”; another frankly admits he expects to “get something for his trouble” without any intention of casting a ballot. These are not isolated rogues but representative figures. The satirical force lies in the gap between the high language of constitutional liberty and the grubby reality of the market. Goldsmith writes:

“The candidates are not so much distinguished by their abilities, as by their purses; he who can spend most, and bestow the greatest number of places and pensions, is sure to be the favourite.”

The election is not a contest of ideas or character; it is an auction. The famous English liberty, held up as a beacon to the world, is reduced to a transaction in which the people sell their birthright for a bellyful of beer and a few shillings.

The essay’s most devastating moment comes when Lien Chi thanks Heaven that he was “born in a country where we have no such liberties.” This ironic inversion is the satirical climax. To prefer the orderly despotism of China to the chaos of an English election is a calculated insult to national pride. It forces the reader to ask what the word ‘liberty’ actually means. Goldsmith implies that true liberty requires civic virtue, rationality, and respect for law—qualities conspicuously absent from the scene he describes. The critic John B. Moore has argued that “Goldsmith’s political satire is conservative in its insistence that order and moral integrity are the prerequisites of genuine freedom.” The essay is not an attack on freedom itself but on the debased version of it that masquerades as liberty in a corrupt polity. The mob violence, the drunkenness, the venality—these are not freedom but license, and Goldsmith exposes them as a betrayal of the very ideals they claim to embody.

The Voters and the Candidates: A Portrait of Mutual Degradation

Goldsmith’s portrayal of the electorate is unsparing. The voters are not independent citizens exercising a sacred right; they are commodities, bought and sold like cattle. The candidates, for their part, are “purses on legs,” men without ideas or principles who debase themselves by pandering to the lowest appetites of the mob. The real election platform, Goldsmith notes, is “the shake of the hand, the board spread with beef and pudding, and the flowing bowl.” This reduction of politics to gastronomy and bribery is both comic and appalling. There is no virtuous party, no honest candidate, no principled voter. The entire electoral ritual is a mutually reinforcing cycle of corruption in which each party degrades the other and the public good is entirely forgotten. As the critic Arthur Friedman observed:

“Goldsmith never forgets that the follies he laughs at are also vices that harm society.”

The laughter in ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is bitter because it recognises that these follies have real consequences: they undermine the legitimacy of representative government and turn the machinery of freedom into a market for the sale of public trust.

## Goldsmith’s Satirical Tone and Moral Purpose

Goldsmith’s satire differs markedly from Addison’s gentle, affectionate humour. Where Addison smiles at Sir Roger’s eccentricities with tender indulgence, Goldsmith lashes the electoral system with a whip of indignation. His tone is one of feigned innocence that conceals a fierce moral anger. The critic Washington Irving captured this blend when he remarked:

“Goldsmith’s satire, while playful in tone, never loses sight of its moral purpose; it laughs men out of their follies rather than flogging them into reform.”

This distinction is crucial. Goldsmith does not merely entertain; he aims to provoke reform by stinging national pride and shaming the English into living up to their own ideals. The essay is a political fable that transcends its immediate context. Its diagnosis of the relationship between money, mob violence, and electoral corruption remains relevant wherever democratic forms are hollowed out by private interests. ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is thus not only a masterwork of eighteenth-century satire but a permanent contribution to the literature of political dissent.

## Conclusion: The Two Faces of the Age

Read together, Addison’s ‘Sir Roger at Home’ and Goldsmith’s ‘Parliamentary Elections’ illuminate the private and public faces of early eighteenth-century England. Addison celebrates the domestic virtues of the old rural order—kindness, loyalty, simple faith—while Goldsmith exposes the brutal venality of the new political order. One is an elegy for a vanishing world; the other a jeremiad against a corrupt present. Both, however, are animated by a common faith in the power of literature to improve society by holding up a mirror to its follies and its virtues. Their continued study is essential for understanding not only the periodical essay as a form but also the enduring tensions between tradition and modernity, liberty and license, and private goodness and public vice.

## Part One: Addison’s ‘Sir Roger at Home’

Examine the character of Sir Roger de Coverley as portrayed in Addison’s ‘Sir Roger at Home’. How does Addison blend humour and affection in his depiction?

## Answer

Joseph Addison’s ‘Sir Roger at Home’ introduces Sir Roger de Coverley as a complex mixture of oddity and virtue. The essay opens with the narrator’s arrival at the country seat, where Sir Roger’s hospitality immediately sets the tone. The servants are not mere hired labour but part of a warm domestic circle, and the old knight treats them like his own children. Sir Roger’s benevolence is the foundation of his character; Addison writes that “his whole family has lived in his house above a dozen years each in their respective stations,” indicating a feudal loyalty that is both admirable and faintly old-fashioned. At the same time, Addison gently exposes Sir Roger’s eccentricities, such as his proud display of the portrait of his great-grandfather “who invented the country-dance.” This comic detail prevents the portrait from becoming sentimental; we smile at the old gentleman’s whimsical priorities even as we warm to his simplicity. The picture gallery thus becomes a symbol of his deep but slightly absurd attachment to the past.

The chaplain episode further deepens the blend of humour and affection. Sir Roger keeps a resident chaplain and genuinely venerates him, yet confesses that he does “not much like his preaching, because it is more like a prayer than a sermon.” He falls asleep during the service yet remains a devout churchman. Addison neither ridicules nor idealises this; he shows a man whose faith is sincere even if his intellect cannot follow theological subtlety. The humour here is tender, never mocking, and it makes Sir Roger’s piety more human. The dinner scene confirms this tone—the conversation is cheerful, the food plain but plentiful, and the atmosphere one of mutual trust. The narrator observes that Sir Roger “preserves the good humour and simplicity of his ancestors.” This closing remark defines the character: he is a relic of a vanishing rural England, lovable in his eccentricity, dignified in his kindness.

The critic William Hazlitt remarked that “the character of Sir Roger de Coverley is the masterpiece of Addison’s genius, a creation that mixes the laughable and the amiable with inimitable grace.” Indeed, Sir Roger is neither a figure of pure satire nor of idealisation; he exists in a middle space where wit and tenderness coexist. Addison makes us laugh at his oddities while simultaneously admiring his fundamental goodness. This dual vision is the secret of the essay’s lasting charm and the reason Sir Roger remains one of the most beloved characters in English periodical literature.

Discuss the theme of domestic harmony and benevolence in Addison’s ‘Sir Roger at Home’.

## Answer

The essay ‘Sir Roger at Home’ presents an idealised vision of rural domestic life built on mutual affection and benevolent patriarchy. From the moment the narrator arrives, he is struck by the warmth that pervades the household: “I was received with the utmost joy and affection by the whole family.” The word ‘family’ here is deliberately chosen to erase the line between kin and servant, suggesting an organic community rather than a contractual arrangement. Sir Roger’s relationship with his old butler and other servants is not one of master and subordinate but of protector and dependent. He never dismisses anyone, and the entire staff has grown grey in his service. This permanence creates a sense of security that Addison clearly values in an age of increasing urban mobility and commercial impersonality. The household becomes a miniature commonwealth governed not by law but by kindness.

This harmony extends to the spiritual realm through the figure of the chaplain. Sir Roger’s treatment of the chaplain is a model of generous patronage. The chaplain lives “very comfortably,” receives a “good annuity for life,” and is treated as a friend. The religious life of the house is simple and consensual; there are no doctrinal quarrels. Sir Roger may doze during sermons, but he never doubts the chaplain’s worth or fails in his duties. The peace of the household is, in part, a reflection of this untroubled faith. However, Addison does not present a perfect utopia. The absence of a mistress leaves the house slightly disordered, and Sir Roger’s intellectual limitations are gently noted. These imperfections make the domestic harmony believable and earned, not artificial. They suggest that benevolence can thrive even without refinement, as long as the heart is in the right place.

The critic Samuel Johnson praised The Spectator for its moral instruction, noting that “it is the first book to teach the common people to think, and to teach them to think justly.” Addison’s portrait of Sir Roger’s home is indeed a moral lesson: it teaches that true harmony arises from loyalty, generosity, and simple faith rather than from wealth or polish. In a rapidly changing society, the essay offers a gentle defence of traditional country values. The household at Coverley Hall is a refuge from the competition and hypocrisy of the town, and its portrait remains one of the most endearing images of domestic peace in English literature.

Analyse the role of religion and the chaplain in ‘Sir Roger at Home’. How does Addison balance piety and humour in this episode?

## Answer

Religion in ‘Sir Roger at Home’ functions as both a moral anchor and a source of gentle comedy. The chaplain, a man “of good sense and some learning,” lives under Sir Roger’s roof as a respected friend rather than a hired cleric. Sir Roger’s patronage is exemplary: he allows the chaplain a comfortable annuity and treats him with genuine reverence. This arrangement reflects an older, integrated model of rural Anglicanism where the squire and the priest cooperated to sustain the spiritual and social order. Yet Addison does not let piety become pompous. He immediately introduces a comic counterpoint through Sir Roger’s confession that he does “not much like his preaching, because it is more like a prayer than a sermon.” The knight, whose faith is more habitual than intellectual, finds his chaplain’s style too meditative for his taste—a wonderfully human detail that punctures any hint of sanctimony.

The humour deepens with the revelation that Sir Roger often falls asleep during the sermon. This could easily have become a satirical attack on hypocritical devotion, but Addison handles it with characteristic lightness. Sir Roger’s slumber is not the result of irreverence; it is simply the physical surrender of an old man in a warm room after a week of country activity. His esteem for the chaplain remains undiminished, and he boasts that “the good man deserves a better livelihood.” The paradox of the snoring squire who yet considers himself a strict churchman is the source of the humour, but the laughter is directed at the foible, not at the faith. Addison makes us see that simple, imperfect piety is still piety, and perhaps more lovable than rigorous doctrinal correctness.

The Anglican divine and critic John Henry Newman once observed that “the Church of England is a serviceable religion for a country gentleman.” Sir Roger embodies this truth perfectly. His religion is of the parish church, the family pew, and the familiar prayer-book, not of theological controversy. The chaplain, too, is content with his easy living and does not demand more spiritual exertion than his patron can provide. This comfortable symbiosis mirrors the ideal of a stable, hierarchical society. By blending piety with humour, Addison avoids both the dryness of mere satire and the heaviness of overt moralising. The result is a portrait of religious life that is tolerant, warm, and recognizably human—a small triumph of the periodical essayist’s art.

Comment on Addison’s narrative technique and the role of the Spectator persona in ‘Sir Roger at Home’.

## Answer

The narrative technique of ‘Sir Roger at Home’ relies on the carefully constructed persona of Mr. Spectator, an urbane, observant, and fundamentally sympathetic narrator. Mr. Spectator is not a character in the drama but a witness, and this slight detachment gives him the freedom to report Sir Roger’s oddities without malice. His tone is that of an amused but affectionate friend. He notices, for instance, the old butler’s joy at Sir Roger’s arrival and remarks, “I could not but observe with a great deal of pleasure, the joy that appeared in the countenances of these ancient domestics.” This is not the voice of a social satirist looking for faults; it is the voice of a guest genuinely moved by the scene. The Spectator persona thus acts as a mediating consciousness, filtering the raw detail of rural life through a lens of cultivated understanding. He translates the provincial world for a London audience without ever condescending to it.

The essay’s structure follows the rhythm of a country visit, moving from arrival to gallery to dinner, and this casual progression reinforces the sense of naturalness. Mr. Spectator does not force conclusions; he lets Sir Roger reveal himself through action and conversation. The description of the picture gallery, with its comical detail of the dancing great-grandfather, is presented simply as observed fact, but the selection of this detail is itself a subtle act of ironic portraiture. The narrator’s occasional reflections—such as when he admires Sir Roger for preserving “the good humour and simplicity of his ancestors”—guide the reader’s response without becoming intrusive. This technique blends the essay form with the emerging novelistic art of characterisation.

Critic C. S. Lewis praised Addison’s prose for its “middle style”—clear, elegant, and suited to the common reader. The Spectator persona embodies this style perfectly. He is neither a pedant nor a buffoon, but a gentleman of taste who can appreciate both the sophistication of the town and the simplicity of the country. Through his eyes, Sir Roger becomes more than a caricature; he becomes a living, breathing figure whose weaknesses only endear him more. The persona thus allows Addison to achieve a delicate balance between instruction and entertainment, making ‘Sir Roger at Home’ a model of the periodical essay as a form of humane social observation.

Discuss Sir Roger de Coverley as a representative of the old English country squire. What values does he embody, and how does Addison regard the passing of this social type?

## Answer

Sir Roger de Coverley stands as a literary monument to the old English country squire, a social type that was already fading when Addison wrote. He embodies the values of benevolent patriarchy, loyalty to tradition, and simple Anglican piety. His household is run on principles of mutual obligation rather than commercial calculation. The servants are “ancient domestics” who have grown old in their places, and Sir Roger feels a personal responsibility for each of them. Addison underlines this by noting that “he seldom dismisses any of them.” This attitude reflects a feudal inheritance in which landownership entails duties as well as privileges. Sir Roger’s pride in his ancestors, particularly the great-grandfather “who invented the country-dance,” shows a deep attachment to the local and the customary. He cherishes continuity, not change, and his home is a repository of family memory.

At the same time, Addison is aware that this type of squire is becoming obsolete in the commercial and political modernity of eighteenth-century England. Sir Roger’s bachelor state and the slight disorder of his house hint at a failure to adapt and renew. His intellectual simplicity, while endearing, also makes him ill-suited for a society increasingly dominated by urban sophistication and parliamentary politics. The essay does not openly mourn, but a note of elegy underlies the affectionate portraiture. When Mr. Spectator praises Sir Roger for preserving the “simplicity of his ancestors,” there is an implicit recognition that this simplicity is under threat. The world of Coverley Hall is a retreat, not a model for the future.

The political philosopher Edmund Burke later famously lamented the passing of “the age of chivalry” and the “little platoon” of local attachments. Addison’s Sir Roger anticipates this elegiac conservatism. The essay suggests that while the old squire’s eccentricities are amusing, his core virtues—kindness, hospitality, and a sense of social duty—are genuinely worth preserving. Addison does not argue for a return to feudalism; he simply holds up a gentle mirror to what is being lost. In doing so, he creates a character who is both a delightful individual and a symbol of a passing social order. This dual significance makes ‘Sir Roger at Home’ a text of considerable historical as well as literary interest, capturing the tension between tradition and modernity that defined the early eighteenth century.

## Part Two: Goldsmith’s ‘Parliamentary Elections’

Analyse Goldsmith’s satire on electoral corruption in ‘Parliamentary Elections’. How does he expose the gap between the ideal of liberty and its reality?

## Answer

Goldsmith’s ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is a devastating satire on the corruption that pervaded eighteenth-century English elections. Through the eyes of the Chinese philosopher Lien Chi Altangi, the essay systematically dismantles the English boast of political liberty. The narrator arrives at the borough expecting to witness rational debate and virtuous citizens, but instead finds “the whole town one continued scene of riot and confusion.” This immediate contradiction between expectation and reality is the central satirical strategy. The ideal of the freeborn Englishman exercising his rights is replaced by the reality of drunken mobs, bribery, and violence. The voters are not independent agents; they are commodities in a political market. One man proudly tells Lien Chi that he has “already sold his vote to both sides, and would vote for neither,” exposing the complete absence of civic principle.

The candidates are equally degraded. Goldsmith observes that they are “not so much distinguished by their abilities, as by their purses.” The election is not a contest of ideas but an auction, with the deepest purse winning. The famous English liberty, held up as a model for the world, is reduced to a commercial transaction. The satirical climax arrives when Lien Chi defines an election as “a kind of a fair, where the principal commodity to be sold is the liberty of the people.” This metaphor is brutally precise. A fair is a place of temporary excitement, noise, and bargaining; liberty, the supposed foundation of the constitution, is the good being haggled over. The sacred right of voting becomes a saleable item, and the people are willing sellers. Goldsmith’s irony is that the English themselves are the auctioneers of their own freedom.

The critic and biographer Washington Irving remarked that “Goldsmith’s satire, while playful in tone, never loses sight of its moral purpose; it laughs men out of their follies rather than flogging them into reform.” In ‘Parliamentary Elections,’ this laughter is sharp and provocative. By using a Chinese observer, Goldsmith forces his English readers to see their political system as a foreigner might—as a bizarre ritual in which noise, money, and mob rule masquerade as liberty. The essay does not propose a remedy, but its diagnosis is unsparing. It remains one of the most effective satirical exposures of electoral malpractice in English literature, demonstrating that the gap between libertarian rhetoric and political reality is as wide as it is scandalous.

Discuss the role and effectiveness of the foreign observer (Lien Chi Altangi) as a satirical device in Goldsmith’s ‘Parliamentary Elections’.

## Answer

The use of a foreign observer is the master stroke of Goldsmith’s satirical method in ‘Parliamentary Elections.’ Lien Chi Altangi, a Chinese philosopher, approaches the English electoral process with the innocent curiosity of a rational outsider. He has heard of English liberty and wishes to see it in action, but what he encounters is a carnival of bribery and violence. This device of defamiliarisation strips the election of its patriotic rhetoric and reveals it as a barbaric ritual. When Lien Chi notes that “the whole town one continued scene of riot and confusion,” he is not exaggerating for effect; he is simply describing what any objective stranger would see. The familiar is made strange, and the absurdity of the proceedings becomes starkly visible. The Chinese perspective acts as a neutral mirror, free from the prejudices that prevent Englishmen from seeing their own corruption.

The effectiveness of this technique lies in its double-edged irony. On one level, Lien Chi’s naivety exposes the gulf between English pretensions and English practice. His literal-minded reports—that voters sell their votes to both sides, that candidates are distinguished by their purses rather than their abilities—are devastating because they cannot be dismissed as partisan. On another level, Goldsmith uses Lien Chi’s Chinese identity to provoke his readers. The philosopher thanks Heaven he was “born in a country where we have no such liberties.” To an English reader in 1762, this preference for Chinese despotism over British freedom was a calculated insult, designed to sting national pride into self-examination. The satire thus works by making the English see themselves as others might see them—not as the champions of liberty but as slaves to corruption and mob rule.

Critic George Saintsbury noted that “Goldsmith’s Chinese letters are the ancestors of all those works in which a foreign visitor holds up the mirror to European society.” The device had been popularised earlier by Montesquieu’s Persian Letters, but Goldsmith adapts it with his characteristic warmth and moral clarity. Lien Chi is not a cold analyst; he is a humane observer genuinely shocked by what he witnesses. His final judgment—that an election is a fair where liberty is sold—carries the force of a moral verdict. By choosing a narrator who stands outside the culture, Goldsmith achieves a perspective that is simultaneously inside the action (as a visitor) and outside its assumptions. This dual position makes the satire inescapable and gives the essay its enduring critical power.

Examine Goldsmith’s portrayal of the voters and candidates in ‘Parliamentary Elections’. How does this portrayal contribute to the overall satire?

## Answer

Goldsmith’s portrayal of voters in ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is unsparingly contemptuous. The electors are depicted as a venal, drunken, and utterly unprincipled mob. They do not vote out of conviction; they sell their suffrage to the highest bidder. The narrator records one voter who “had already sold his vote to both sides, and would vote for neither,” and another who frankly admits he has no intention of voting but expects to “get something for his trouble.” These are not isolated rogues but representative figures of the electorate. The implication is that the entire system rests on a foundation of greed and dishonesty. The voters are treated by the candidates as purchasable commodities, and they eagerly accept this role. Goldsmith’s satire gains its bite from the contrast between the Enlightenment ideal of the rational, independent citizen and the brutish reality he describes. The dignity of the franchise is completely absent.

The candidates fare no better. They are not men of ideas, policies, or public spirit; they are purses on legs. Goldsmith states bluntly that they are “not so much distinguished by their abilities, as by their purses.” They win office not by persuading the electorate but by outspending their rivals on beer, beef, and bribes. The “shake of the hand, the board spread with beef and pudding, and the flowing bowl” constitute the real election platform. This reduces the political process to a gastronomic and financial contest, devoid of intellectual or moral content. The candidates debase themselves as much as the voters, creating a symbiosis of corruption in which each degrades the other. The whole electoral ritual becomes a mutually reinforcing cycle of venality.

By portraying both voters and candidates in this light, Goldsmith creates a closed, airless world of moral squalor from which principle has been entirely evacuated. The satirical effect is one of suffocation; there is no virtuous alternative within the system. This grim picture serves Goldsmith’s larger purpose of questioning the very myth of English liberty. As the critic Arthur Friedman observed, “Goldsmith never forgets that the follies he laughs at are also vices that harm society.” The portrayal of voters and candidates is not merely comic exaggeration; it is a serious indictment of a political culture that has betrayed its own ideals. The laughter it provokes is bitter because it forces the reader to confront the possibility that the machinery of freedom may be nothing more than a market for the sale of public trust.

How does Goldsmith use the theme of ‘liberty versus license’ in ‘Parliamentary Elections’ to criticise the English political system?

## Answer

The theme of liberty versus license is at the heart of Goldsmith’s satirical attack in ‘Parliamentary Elections.’ The English of the eighteenth century prided themselves on being the freest people in Europe, with a parliamentary system that protected the rights of the subject. Goldsmith, through his Chinese observer, systematically dismantles this self-image by showing that what the English call liberty is in fact license—the unbridled freedom of the mob to act without restraint. The election is “one continued scene of riot and confusion,” a place where “every hour I expected to see them cut each other’s throats.” This is not the orderly exercise of political rights; it is anarchy. The philosophical distinction between liberty (freedom within the bounds of law and morality) and license (freedom without moral or legal limit) is played out in the streets of the borough. The voters are free—to drink, to fight, to sell their votes—but they are not free citizens in any meaningful republican sense.

Goldsmith sharpens the critique by having Lien Chi thank Heaven that he was born in a country “where we have no such liberties.” This ironic inversion is the key satirical moment. By equating English liberty with throat-cutting chaos, and preferring the orderly despotism of China, Goldsmith forces the reader to ask what the word ‘liberty’ actually means. If liberty merely consists in the right to be bought and to brawl, then it is a worthless thing. The essay suggests that true liberty requires civic virtue, rationality, and respect for law—qualities utterly absent from the electoral scene. Goldsmith implies that the English have mistaken license for liberty and that their boast of freedom is a hollow slogan disguising a corrupt oligarchy manipulated by money and mob violence.

The critic John B. Moore has argued that “Goldsmith’s political satire is conservative in its insistence that order and moral integrity are the prerequisites of genuine freedom.” This reading aligns with the essay’s implicit message. Goldsmith does not reject liberty as such; he rejects the debased version of it that he witnesses. The chaos of the election is not an argument for tyranny but a call to reform. The satirical exposure of license parading as liberty is designed to shame the English into living up to their own ideals. By dramatising the gap between the language of freedom and the reality of corruption, Goldsmith writes a political fable that remains relevant wherever elections are bought and sold.

Compare the satirical methods of Addison in ‘Sir Roger at Home’ and Goldsmith in ‘Parliamentary Elections’. How do their different tones serve their respective purposes?

## Answer

Addison and Goldsmith both employ satire to examine English society, but their methods and tones differ fundamentally. Addison’s satire in ‘Sir Roger at Home’ is gentle, affectionate, and corrective rather than punitive. He portrays Sir Roger de Coverley with a smile, noting his eccentricities—the pride in the dancing great-grandfather, the dozing in church—without ever letting the laughter become cruel. The humour is designed to warm the reader toward the character, not to distance or condemn him. Addison’s purpose is moral instruction through sympathetic portraiture; he wants his audience to laugh at Sir Roger’s foibles and yet love him for his virtues. The irony is soft, the criticism implied rather than stated. For instance, when Mr. Spectator admires Sir Roger for preserving “the good humour and simplicity of his ancestors,” the remark is both a compliment and a gentle hint that such simplicity is vanishing. Addison’s tone is elegiac and consoling.

Goldsmith’s satire in ‘Parliamentary Elections’ is far sharper and more indignant. He uses the foreign observer Lien Chi Altangi not to sympathise but to expose. The tone is one of mock-innocence that conceals a fierce moral anger. When Lien Chi defines an election as “a kind of a fair, where the principal commodity to be sold is the liberty of the people,” the metaphor is devastating precisely because it is delivered with apparent calm. Goldsmith’s purpose is not to make us love the electoral mob but to make us ashamed of it. His laughter is bitter, aiming to provoke reform by stinging national pride. Unlike Addison, who works within the domestic sphere and finds humanity in imperfection, Goldsmith operates in the public sphere and finds corruption that he believes must be rooted out.

The critic Austin Dobson noted that “Addison is a friendly censor, Goldsmith an angry lover of his country.” This distinction captures the essence of their satirical divergence. Addison’s Sir Roger is a lovable anachronism, a private gentleman whose flaws are harmless; Goldsmith’s voters and candidates are public menaces whose venality corrupts the state. Addison consoles his readers with the image of a benevolent old England; Goldsmith alarms them with the spectacle of a debased new England. Together, the two essays demonstrate the range of eighteenth-century satire, from the warm domestic humour of The Spectator to the biting political irony of The Citizen of the World. Both are effective, but they appeal to different emotions—Addison to affection, Goldsmith to indignation—and in doing so, they illuminate the private and public faces of their age.
