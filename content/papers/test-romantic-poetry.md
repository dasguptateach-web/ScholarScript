## Romantic Poetry: The Sublime in Nature

Introduction
The Romantic period in English literature (1798-1837) marked a profound shift in how poets understood and represented nature. This paper examines the concept of the sublime in the poetry of William Wordsworth, Samuel Taylor Coleridge, and John Keats.

Wordsworth's Nature
William Wordsworth's poetry presents nature as a living, breathing entity that shapes human consciousness. In "Tintern Abbey," he describes nature as "the anchor of my purest thoughts, the nurse, the guide, the guardian of my heart, and soul of all my moral being."

Coleridge's Supernatural
Samuel Taylor Coleridge's "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner" blends natural and supernatural elements. The mariner's journey represents a fall from grace and a redemption through the appreciation of natural beauty.

Keats and Negative Capability
John Keats developed the concept of "negative capability" - the ability to exist in uncertainty without reaching for fact or reason. His "Ode to a Nightingale" exemplifies this approach, finding beauty in the tension between joy and mortality.

Conclusion
The Romantic poets transformed English literature by making nature not just a setting but a central character in the drama of human consciousness.
