## JONATHAN SWIFT, GULLIVER’S TRAVELS BOOKS I & II COMPLETE STUDY GUIDE

Why This Guide?

This guide covers the West Bengal State University (WBSU) English Honours Semester 4 syllabus for Module IV: Jonathan Swift’s Gulliver’s Travels, Books I (A Voyage to Lilliput) and II (A Voyage to Brobdingnag). It contains detailed plot summaries of both books, an analysis of major themes, character sketches, key quotations, and exam-oriented questions with answer structures—all written in fluid, human language to help you prepare effectively for your examination.

## 1. Jonathan Swift and Gulliver’s Travels: Brief Background

Jonathan Swift (1667–1745) was an Anglo-Irish poet, essayist, and political writer, immortalised in English literature for his razor-sharp satire. Gulliver’s Travels (full title: Travels into Several Remote Nations of the World, in Four Parts. By Lemuel Gulliver, First a Surgeon, and then a Captain of Several Ships) was published in 1726. It works simultaneously as an adventure tale, a social satire, and a brutal analysis of human nature. Books I and II hold up a reversed telescope to European politics, religious bigotry, and moral decay through the lenses of contrasting scale. Through the tiny Lilliputians, Swift ridicules the Whig–Tory conflicts, the Catholic–Protestant divide, and the wars between England and France. In Brobdingnag, the land of giants, the exaggerated human body and the limitations of European knowledge and morality are exposed with savage honesty.

## 2. Book I – A Voyage to Lilliput: Detailed Plot Summary

Lemuel Gulliver begins his narrative by telling us that he is the third of five sons of a modest Nottinghamshire family. He studied at Emanuel College, Cambridge, and was later apprenticed to a London surgeon, Mr. James Bates. His fascination with travel and navigation made him take up service as a ship’s surgeon. After a brief marriage and a failed medical practice, financial necessity drives him back to sea. On 4 May 1699, he sails from Bristol aboard the Antelope, bound for the South Seas. Disaster strikes during a violent storm: the ship hits a rock and splits apart. Gulliver and a few sailors manage a lifeboat, but a sudden squall overturns it. He swims for his life, eventually finding shallow water and a shore. Collapsing on the soft grass, he falls into an exhausted sleep.

When he awakes, he finds himself unable to move. His arms, legs and even his long hair have been fastened to the ground by hundreds of tiny threads. The sun rises, and he feels something moving across his body: a small human creature, not six inches tall, walks on his chest. Gulliver roars in astonishment, scattering the little people, but they soon return. He strains to break his bonds, and when he frees his left arm, the creatures shoot a volley of tiny arrows into his hand and face, stinging like needles. Deciding it is safer to lie still, he signals for food and drink. The Lilliputians understand his gestures: hundreds of them bring ladders and hoist up baskets of meat and loaves no bigger than musket bullets, and two hogsheads of delicious wine. Gulliver consumes whole herds of cattle at a meal, much to the wonder of his hosts. Exhausted, he later discovers that the Lilliputians have mixed a sleeping draught in his wine, causing him to fall into another deep slumber while they prepare to transport him.

While he sleeps, a team of five hundred carpenters and engineers constructs a massive wooden frame on twenty-two wheels. With enormous effort, they roll Gulliver onto this engine, tie him securely, and drag him half a league to the capital city, Mildendo. There he is chained inside a huge, disused temple outside the metropolis—large enough for him to creep inside and lie down. The Emperor himself, accompanied by his court, visits Gulliver and stands on a turret to address him. Though the language is incomprehensible, Gulliver is impressed by the Emperor’s grace. Soon, scholars are appointed to teach him the Lilliputian language, and he progresses rapidly, enabling him to ask for his liberty. Meanwhile, officials conduct a meticulous inventory of his pockets. They catalogue a collection of “wonderful machines”: a scimitar (his sword), a pair of pocket pistols with powder and shot, a silver snuff-box, a watch that makes a continual noise (which they suspect is a god), a comb, a handkerchief, a purse of gold and silver, and other curiosities. The inventory is written in the language of the empire, full of grandiloquent phrases that satirize bureaucratic pomposity. Gulliver voluntarily surrenders his sword and pistols, firing one into the air to the terror of the crowd, which to them sounds like a thunderclap.

Gulliver’s mild behaviour gradually wins the Emperor’s favour. Soldiers and citizens come daily to watch him, and he entertains them by letting them dance on his hand. Eventually, he petitions for freedom. The Emperor, after consulting his council, draws up a set of nine conditions which Gulliver must swear to observe. These include: not to leave the dominions without a licence; not to enter the metropolis without warning the inhabitants two hours beforehand; to walk only along the main roads and not trample fields; to carry important messengers in his pocket; to ally with the Emperor against the hostile island of Blefuscu and, when required, to destroy the enemy’s fleet; and to assist the workmen in lifting heavy stones for the royal buildings. Gulliver agrees joyfully, and his chains are struck off.

Once free, Gulliver explores the kingdom and learns about its political state. His friend the principal secretary Reldresal explains the internal strife and the great war with Blefuscu. The conflict, Swift’s sublime parody of religious and political schism, arises from a seemingly trivial dispute: which end of a boiled egg should be broken before eating. Traditionally, eggs were broken at the larger end, but the present Emperor’s grandfather, as a child, cut his finger on the large end, prompting his father to issue an edict commanding all Lilliputians to break their eggs at the smaller end. This caused massive rebellion, with those who refused (the “Big-Endians”) fleeing to Blefuscu, which now threatens invasion. The religious allegory refers to the Catholic-Protestant conflict over transubstantiation and the endless wars between England and France.

The Emperor, knowing Gulliver’s strength, implores him to help in the war. Gulliver wades across the narrow channel separating the two empires, ingeniously protecting his eyes from Blefuscudian arrows with spectacles. Arriving at the enemy fleet, he cuts the cables of their fifty warships with a stout cable and hook, ties them together, and drags the whole fleet back to Lilliput. This spectacular victory forces Blefuscu to sue for peace, and Gulliver is honoured with the highest title in the kingdom, “Nardac.” But this triumph sows the seeds of his fall. The Emperor, inflated with ambition, demands that Gulliver now reduce Blefuscu to a province and crush the Big-Endian exiles completely. Gulliver, adhering to his own moral code, refuses, stating that he “would never be an instrument of bringing a free and brave people into slavery.” This principled stand enrages the court.

Further trouble follows an accidental act that, though heroic, earns the permanent hatred of the Empress. During a fire in the royal apartments, Gulliver, seeing that the palace is about to be destroyed and that buckets of water would be useless, urinates over the building in a torrent that extinguishes the blaze within minutes. He saves the entire structure and the Empress’s life, but she is so mortified by the indignity that she retreats to a distant part of the palace and vows vengeance. A law is actually enacted forbidding any person from urinating within the palace precincts, and the Empress’s enmity becomes a driving force behind the plot against Gulliver.

Soon, a conspiracy coalesces. The Admiral Skyresh Bolgolam, jealous of Gulliver’s glory, and Flimnap, the High Treasurer, who suspects an affair between Gulliver and his wife (a bizarre misinterpretation of a harmless visit), join with other ministers. They draw up articles of impeachment, charging Gulliver with high treason for refusing the Emperor’s command regarding Blefuscu, for entering the palace without licence to extinguish the fire, and for sundry other “capital offences.” The penalty proposed is nothing less than having his eyes put out—a punishment considered merciful by the council since they fear he might break his chains if executed outright. A friendly courtier secretly visits Gulliver at night, outlines the entire scheme, and urges him to prepare his defence. Gulliver realises the gravity of his position: the Emperor’s vanity and the malice of his ministers leave no room for justice. Without waiting for a formal hearing, he flees to Blefuscu, where he is warmly received as a hero.

In Blefuscu, Gulliver stays in honourable retirement, but he longs to return home. Walking along the shore one day, he discovers an overturned boat of normal human proportions, evidently washed in from the ocean. The Emperor of Blefuscu grants him permission to use it, and thousands of workers assist Gulliver in repairing the vessel. They equip it with sails, a mast, and provisions: the carcasses of a hundred oxen and three hundred sheep, bread, drink, and cooking utensils. On 24 September 1701, he sets sail from Blefuscu. Two days later, he is picked up by an English merchant ship returning from Japan. The captain, incredulous at first, listens to Gulliver’s story and examines the tiny live cattle and sheep he has brought as souvenirs. Gulliver sells these miniature animals in England for a good sum before settling once more with his family. Thus ends the first voyage—a witty, caustic mirror held up to the absurdities of power, pomp, and petty conflict.

## 3. Book II – A Voyage to Brobdingnag: Detailed Plot Summary

Gulliver, restless as ever, does not stay long at home. Barely two months after his return from Lilliput, he sets out again on 20 June 1702 aboard the Adventure, bound for the East Indies. The ship enjoys favourable winds until they encounter a violent monsoon near the Cape of Good Hope, which drives them far off course. When they finally sight an unknown coast, the captain sends a party ashore in a longboat to search for fresh water. Gulliver, ever curious, joins them. They find no river, only barren rocks. While the sailors walk about, Gulliver strays inland to explore. Suddenly, he sees the crew rowing frantically back to the ship, pursued by a gigantic human being wading through the sea. Terrified, Gulliver runs deep into a field of enormous wheat, where the stalks rise forty feet high. Climbing a steep hill, he stumbles into an extraordinary world: everything is on a colossal scale—thick grass like trees, and hedges so tall he cannot see over them.

He is soon discovered by a party of giant reapers, each as tall as a church steeple. One of them plucks Gulliver up between his thumb and forefinger and examines him. Petrified, Gulliver begs for mercy. The reaper, taking him to be some kind of small animal, wraps him carefully in a handkerchief and carries him to the farmer who owns the land. The farmer, measuring about sixty feet in height, brings Gulliver home and shows him to his family. The farmer’s wife shrieks at first, but soon develops a fondness for him. Their nine-year-old daughter, Glumdalclitch (meaning “little nurse”), becomes his devoted caretaker. She is forty feet tall but gentle and quick-witted. She teaches him the Brobdingnagian language, sews clothes for him from fine linen, and watches over him with motherly affection.

The farmer, however, soon sees the little creature as a source of profit. Acting on the advice of a greedy friend, he takes Gulliver to the nearest market town and exhibits him for money. Gulliver is placed on a table in the marketplace and made to perform various tricks: he salutes the crowd, drinks a thimbleful of wine, and waves a tiny sword (a toothpick-sized blade). The spectacle draws huge crowds, and the farmer rakes in money, forcing Gulliver to perform multiple shows a day until he is utterly exhausted, losing weight and almost dying from fatigue. To capitalise further, the farmer takes him on a tour to the metropolis, Lorbrulgrud (the pride of the universe). There, Gulliver is exhibited at an inn, performing so frequently that his health breaks down entirely. The farmer, calculating that he is likely to die soon, resolves to squeeze out the last bit of profit by raising the price of admission.

Fortunately, word of the tiny wonder reaches the royal court. The Queen is intrigued, and Gulliver is brought before her. She is immediately charmed by his delicate manners and his respectful address. When the Queen asks him whether he would prefer to stay at court, he gladly accepts, begging to be taken away from the exploitative farmer. The Queen purchases Gulliver for a handsome sum and installs him, along with Glumdalclitch, in the palace. From then on, Gulliver lives in a specially crafted travelling box, about twelve feet square, furnished with miniature furniture, a bed, and a lock against intruders.

At court, Gulliver’s life becomes a series of bizarre adventures arising from his minuscule size. Once, while he is eating, a couple of enormous rats climb into his box and attack him; he draws his sword (a tiny scimitar) and fights them off, killing one and driving away the other. On another occasion, a wasp flies in, buzzing like a thousand organ pipes, and Gulliver has to be rescued by Glumdalclitch. The Queen’s dwarf, a malicious and jealous creature, dips Gulliver into a bowl of cream, nearly drowning him, and later sticks him, up to the waist, into a marrow bone. A monkey seizes Gulliver, mistaking him for an infant monkey, carries him onto the roof, and tries to feed him; the poor little man is rescued by the household guards amidst much laughter. Gulliver also has the uncomfortable honour of being undressed and observed by the Maids of Honour, who treat him as a living doll, sometimes placing him on their naked bodies—a description Swift uses to highlight the grotesque physicality of the human form when magnified.

Gulliver, however, yearns for intellectual conversation. The King of Brobdingnag, an enlightened and thoughtful monarch, frequently summons him to discuss the institutions, laws, and manners of Europe. Gulliver, swelling with patriotism, gives the King a detailed and glowing account of the English constitution, Parliament, courts of law, military power, and the history of his native land. The King listens patiently, notes down every detail, and then, over a series of audiences, asks piercing questions. He is horrified by the corruption, venality, and bloodshed Gulliver describes. He probes into political parties, religious dissent, and economic systems. Gulliver, eager to impress, even explains the mechanics of gunpowder and the destructive capacity of European artillery, offering to teach the Brobdingnagians how to manufacture cannons and bombs to subdue their enemies. The King recoils in disgust, forbidding Gulliver ever to mention such infernal inventions again. His final judgment, delivered with chilling calm, is that the natives of Gulliver’s country are “the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth.” Gulliver, stung by this verdict, reflects on how perspective alters value: what seems splendid and glorious in England appears petty, brutal, and contemptible when laid before a wise and morally upright giant king.

In this giant world, Gulliver’s physical insignificance undercuts any pride he had. He is constantly menaced by falling apples, hailstones the size of tennis balls, and hungry birds. In one terrifying incident, a playful spaniel picks him up in its mouth and drops him, half-drowned, at the Queen’s feet. Yet there are also moments of sublime wonder: Gulliver marvels at the beauty of the cosmos when he is carried to the top of a mountain from which he can gaze at the stars without atmospheric distortion, and he is awed by the intricate carpentry and music of the giant court.

After about two years at court, Gulliver accompanies the royal couple on a tour to the south coast. He travels inside his travelling box, carried by a page. During a stop at the seaside, the page sets the box down on a rock and wanders off to collect bird’s eggs. Suddenly, an eagle, attracted by the shiny roof of the box, seizes it in its beak, soars into the air, and carries Gulliver across the ocean. A second eagle challenges the first, and in the scuffle, the box is dropped into the sea. Inside, Gulliver is tossed about violently, seawater seeping through a crack. He cries for help and, after what seems an eternity, hears a grating sound above his head—a rope is being dragged across the roof. He manages to shout out in English, and to his astonishment, a sailor’s voice answers. The box has been sighted by an English ship returning from the East Indies. The crew hoist the box aboard, use a saw to cut a hole in the roof, and pull out the bewildered Gulliver. He explains his residence in Brobdingnag, but the sailors find the tale unbelievable until he shows them the miniature furniture and a giant-sized comb given by Glumdalclitch. They are forced to concede, but Gulliver himself finds the transition disorienting. Having lived among giants, the normal-sized world feels like a “Lilliput” to him; he stumbles about, afraid to tread on people, and it takes him weeks to readjust to his own species. He arrives home to his wife and children in England, but the experience leaves a permanent mark on his sense of human proportion and moral judgment.

## 4. Major Themes and Satire

4.1 Political Satire
The Emperor of Lilliput, his ministers, factions, and the war with Blefuscu directly satirise early eighteenth-century English politics. The Emperor’s lust for power, Flimnap’s rope-dancing, and the petty scramble for office mirror the Whig party under Robert Walpole. The egg-end dispute stands for the Catholic–Protestant conflict over transubstantiation and the wars between England and France.

4.2 Relativity of Perspective
“Nothing is great or little otherwise than by comparison.” This idea is central to both books. In Lilliput, Gulliver is a giant, yet his power is helpless against tiny political machinations. In Brobdingnag, he is an insect, and the glory of England appears as vermin’s mischief. Swift uses scale to mock human arrogance and show the limits of perception.

4.3 Body and Morality
In Lilliput, a bodily function (urination) changes history. In Brobdingnag, magnified skin, pores, and smells reveal the hidden ugliness of the human body that social politeness normally conceals. The King’s verdict that Europeans are “odious vermin” links physical repulsion to moral condemnation.

4.4 Critique of Knowledge and Warfare
Gulliver’s eager description of gunpowder to the Brobdingnagian King provokes revulsion. Swift attacks the pride of European “civilisation,” which invents only destruction. The peaceful, rational Brobdingnag functions as a moral mirror against European militarism.

4.5 Language and Power
The Lilliputian inventory and articles of impeachment are written in ludicrously inflated language, satirising bureaucratic pomposity. How easily such official gravity is overturned by Gulliver’s actions becomes a running joke about power and its pretensions.

## 5. Character Sketches

Lemuel Gulliver: A practical, travel-obsessed realist and a blindly patriotic narrator. His reliability is compromised not by lying but by moral obtuseness; he fails to see the full implications of what he describes. In Lilliput he stands on principle (refusing to enslave Blefuscu), but in Brobdingnag his national pride is shattered by the King’s judgment.

The Emperor of Lilliput: Vain, power-hungry, and small-minded in vengeance. His treatment of Gulliver depends entirely on utility. He awards the title “Nardac” and then cheerfully agrees to his blinding.

Flimnap (High Treasurer): A satirical portrait of Robert Walpole, his rope-dancing agility stands for political sycophancy and jobbery. His suspicion of an affair with his wife mocks the rumour-mongering and jealousy of court life.

Skyresh Bolgolam (High Admiral): Jealous of Gulliver’s naval triumph, he becomes a prime mover behind the impeachment, embodying the spite of a humiliated rival.

The Empress of Lilliput: Mortally offended by the urination incident, she personifies the pride and vengefulness of royalty over a purely bodily embarrassment.

The Brobdingnagian Farmer: A greedy, callous exhibitor who treats Gulliver as a commodity, nearly working him to death for profit—a parable of capitalist exploitation.

Glumdalclitch: The gentle, affectionate nine-year-old giantess who is Gulliver’s devoted nurse and protector, representing genuine human kindness in contrast to the farmer’s greed.

The King of Brobdingnag: An enlightened, inquisitive ruler whose rigorous questioning and final judgment on European society serve as Swift’s own moral voice. He is powerful but not tyrannical, and his revulsion at gunpowder is the ethical climax of Book II.

The Queen of Brobdingnag: Curious, kindly, and intelligent, she sees Gulliver as a sensitive creature rather than a mere curiosity.

The Dwarf: Malicious and jealous, he torments Gulliver because the latter is now even smaller than himself—a cruel study in how the oppressed may themselves oppress when given a chance.

## THREE HIGH-SCORING ESSAY-TYPE QUESTIONS WITH ANSWERS

Question 1:
Analyse Swift’s use of political satire in Book I of Gulliver’s Travels, focusing on the Lilliputian court, the Big-Endian/Little-Endian dispute, and Gulliver’s impeachment. How far does this miniature world reflect the political and religious conflicts of early eighteenth-century England?

## Answer:

Gulliver’s Travels opens with a voyage that, beneath its playful surface, functions as one of the most coruscating political satires in the English language. By shrinking the arena of power to six-inch figures, Swift does not make politics adorable; he makes its petty rivalries, sycophancy, and ideological violence appear exactly as they are—diminished and contemptible. Book I holds up a miniature mirror to early eighteenth-century England, reflecting the Whig–Tory struggle, the Hanoverian succession anxiety, and the enduring wound of the Reformation. The critic John Traugott rightly observes, “The Lilliputians are a grotesque reduction of humanity, but their vices are exactly those of the European powers.” Every detail of the Lilliputian court, from rope-dancing to the egg-breaking schism, re-enacts a specific historical grievance, and Gulliver’s eventual impeachment dramatizes the ingratitude that Swift believed was woven into the fabric of monarchical government.

Swift wastes no time in targeting the mechanics of court preferment. Early in the narrative, Reldresal explains how high offices are filled:

“This diversion is only practised by those persons who are candidates for great employments, and high favour, at court. They are trained in this art from their youth, and are not always of noble birth, or liberal education. When a great office is vacant, either by death or disgrace (which often happens), five or six of those candidates petition the emperor to entertain his majesty and the court with a dance on the rope; and whoever jumps the highest, without falling, succeeds in the office.”

The rope-dancing is transparently Swift’s parody of Walpole’s dexterous political survival and the undignified scrambling for ministerial posts under George I. Flimnap, the High Treasurer, is the most agile dancer, a transparent portrait of Robert Walpole himself. The satirical sting lies in the fact that such gravity-defying antics have nothing to do with governing well; they reward mere physical showmanship, a charge Swift levels at an administration he believed prized cunning over virtue.

The most devastating political allegory, however, is the long-standing war between Lilliput and Blefuscu, which Swift deliberately anchors in a sublime triviality—the correct end at which to break a boiled egg. The origin of the quarrel is narrated with deadpan solemnity:

“It is allowed on all hands, that the primitive way of breaking eggs before we eat them, was upon the larger end: but his present Majesty’s grandfather, while he was a boy, going to eat an egg, and breaking it according to the ancient practice, happened to cut one of his fingers. Whereupon the Emperor his father published an edict, commanding all his subjects, upon great penalties, to break the smaller end. The people so highly resented this law, that our histories tell us, there have been six rebellions raised on that account; wherein one emperor lost his life, and another his crown.”

Here Swift ridicules the Catholic–Protestant schism over transubstantiation, reducing doctrinal difference to breakfast etiquette. Blefuscu stands for France, sheltering the “Big-Endian” exiles just as Louis XIV sheltered the Jacobites after 1688. The passage’s brilliance lies in its sober tone; Swift never winks, allowing the reader to feel the full absurdity of bloodshed over such a non-issue. By making the conflict hinge on an egg, he strips religious war of its sanctimonious aura, exposing it as obstinate tribalism dressed in theological language.

The impeachment of Gulliver completes the satirical portrait of a corrupt state. After Gulliver heroically extinguishes the palace fire by urinating on it, the Empress, mortified by the indignity, becomes his implacable enemy. The articles of impeachment charge him with “high treason” for, among other things, “open breach of the said law, under colour of extinguishing the fire… did make water within the precincts of the said royal palace.” The penalty proposed—blinding—is presented as an act of mercy, since outright execution might provoke resistance. In this darkly comic episode, Swift illustrates how court intrigue, female prudery, and ministerial jealousy swiftly transform a national saviour into a condemned criminal. The episode mirrors the precariousness of political favour in the age of George I, where yesterday’s hero could find himself in the Tower tomorrow. By compressing European realpolitik into the dimensions of a toy kingdom, Swift lets his reader see that the machinations of the mighty are, in truth, a Lilliputian dance, no less ridiculous for being lethal.

Question 2:
Examine the theme of perspective and the relativity of human importance in Gulliver’s Travels, Books I and II. How does Swift manipulate physical scale to expose the moral and physical frailties of mankind?

## Answer:

The first two voyages of Gulliver’s Travels conduct a ruthless experiment in perspective. Swift places his narrator first among pygmies and then among giants, using the shock of altered scale to challenge every comfortable assumption about human grandeur. Size, in this fictional universe, becomes a moral litmus test: what looks magnificent from one angle appears grotesque or pitiful from another. The critic C. J. Rawson captures this design perfectly: “Swift uses changes in scale to create a vertigo of relativity, where man is neither the measure of all things nor particularly admirable.” Together, Lilliput and Brobdingnag demonstrate that human pride is a trick of the eye—an optical illusion sustained only by never seeing oneself from the outside.

In Lilliput, Gulliver initially experiences the intoxication of disproportionate power. To the six-inch inhabitants, he is a “Quinbus Flestrin,” the Man-Mountain, whose single stride traverses their highways and whose appetite consumes entire herds. Gulliver’s physical superiority allows him to perform godlike feats, most spectacularly the capture of the Blefuscudian fleet. Yet this very magnitude proves a snare. The Lilliputians’ envy and fear are not dwarfed by his size; on the contrary, the tiny creatures weave a conspiracy that nearly costs him his eyes. Gulliver’s mass, so overwhelming on the physical plane, offers no protection against the microscopic toxins of political malice. The episode whispers an uncomfortable truth: human vices—envy, ingratitude, ambition—are not diminished by the puniness of their possessors. Lilliput thus uses the giant perspective to reveal that moral squalor can flourish at any scale, and that being physically large does not make a being large-souled.

When Gulliver sails to Brobdingnag, the telescope is reversed, and the inspection of humanity becomes painfully intimate. Here everything that is normally hidden by the polite distances of English society is enlarged to the point of horror. Swift lingers on the texture of skin seen through a magnifying lens. The Maids of Honour, though beautiful by giant standards, subject Gulliver to an unsettlingly corporeal experience:

“They would often strip me naked from top to toe, and lay me at full length in their bosoms… but I am now convinced that this was all a matter of course, for I was no more regarded than a Lilliputian would be among us. I cannot say I was at all pleased with this usage, for the smell of their skins was so strong, and the sight of their bodies, so much magnified, showed every speck and discolouration to the greatest disadvantage.”

Here the female body, conventionally celebrated in love poetry, is rendered repulsive simply through proximity and scale. Swift’s point is not misogynistic but broadly satirical: all human flesh, stripped of romantic illusion and viewed up close, is a catalogue of pores, blemishes, and odours. The giant perspective refuses to let the reader retreat into flattering abstraction; it insists that the emperor has no clothes, and neither does the human body.

The moral culmination of this reversed perspective is the King of Brobdingnag’s interrogation. Gulliver, hoping to dazzle the monarch, delivers a long, self-congratulatory account of English laws, wars, and political institutions. The King listens, notes everything, and then delivers an assessment that still stings after three centuries. Swift gives him one of the most devastating speeches in the book:

“He was perfectly astonished with the historical account I gave him of our affairs during the last century; protesting it was only an heap of conspiracies, rebellions, murders, massacres, revolutions, banishments; the very worst effects that avarice, faction, hypocrisy, perfidiousness, cruelty, rage, madness, hatred, envy, lust, malice, and ambition could produce.”

Then comes the ultimate verdict: “I cannot but conclude the bulk of your natives to be the most pernicious race of little odious vermin that nature ever suffered to crawl upon the surface of the earth.” The King’s judgement arises directly from the altered perspective; from his enormous height, the distinctions of rank, civilisation, and national glory that so impress Gulliver shrink into a uniform spectacle of vicious, scurrying insects. By moving his hero from giant among pygmies to vermin among giants, Swift forces his readers to confront the possibility that human importance is not an absolute but a function of where one stands. In that vertigo of relativity, pride cannot long survive.

Question 3:
Discuss the role of Gulliver as narrator and satiric instrument in the first two voyages. To what extent is he a reliable reporter, and how does Swift exploit his limitations to critique European civilisation and human vanity?

## Answer:

Lemuel Gulliver is one of literature’s most cunningly constructed narrators—a plain, factual man whose very sincerity becomes the sharpest weapon in Swift’s satiric arsenal. On the surface, he presents himself as a trustworthy recorder, meticulous to the point of tedium about navigation, measurements, and inventories. Yet this stolid reliability is precisely what enables the satire to bite: Gulliver reports the absurdities of Lilliput and the moral enormities of Brobdingnag without ever quite grasping their implications for his own world. The critic Ricardo Quintana remarks, “Gulliver is Swift’s supreme ironic creation, a plain man whose literal-mindedness allows the full horror of what he describes to dawn upon the reader, even as Gulliver himself remains morally obtuse.” In Books I and II, the gap between what Gulliver sees and what the reader understands becomes the primary engine of Swift’s critique, targeting not only political institutions but the blind pride with which Europeans regarded their civilisation.

In Book I, Gulliver’s documentary impulse is on full display during the inventory of his pockets. The Lilliputian officers catalogue his possessions in a language of grand bureaucratic pomposity, describing his watch thus: “A wonderful kind of engine… we took for a deity, because it made a continual noise.” Gulliver transcribes this report without a flicker of irony, presenting it as a curious fact. The reader, however, immediately perceives the satire on human attempts to comprehend the unfamiliar through superstitious reverence. Gulliver’s narrative deadpan allows the Lilliputians to indict themselves, while he remains a neutral lens. He never pauses to remark, “Is this not how we, too, treat strange customs?” That silence is the space in which Swift’s irony grows.

Gulliver’s reliability cracks most tellingly when his patriotic fervour collides with the Brobdingnagian perspective. Invited to describe his native land to the King, Gulliver rises to the occasion with a performance of uncritical national pride. He enumerates the English constitution, the wisdom of Parliament, the splendour of the courts, and even—with alarming enthusiasm—the mechanics of gunpowder and artillery, offering to teach the giants how to manufacture cannon. The King is horrified, but Gulliver misreads the reaction as mere provincial ignorance. After the King’s damning judgement, Gulliver does not reconsider his own values; instead, he rationalises the verdict away with spectacular condescension:

“But great allowances should be given to a king who lives wholly secluded from the rest of the world, and must therefore be altogether unacquainted with the manners and customs that most prevail in other nations: the want of which knowledge will ever produce many prejudices, and a certain narrowness of thinking.”

This passage is a masterclass in ironic narration. The reader understands that the King’s “narrowness of thinking” is in fact a profound moral clarity, while Gulliver’s cosmopolitan “allowances” are the very prejudices that justify colonialism, warfare, and exploitation. Swift does not need to step onto the page to condemn Gulliver; he simply lets the narrator’s own words damn him. Gulliver emerges as an unreliable narrator not because he lies, but because he is incapable of moral self-awareness.

Yet Gulliver is not merely a satiric butt; he also serves as an everyman whose gradual diminishment mirrors the reader’s own journey. In Lilliput, he is a confident giant, certain of his benevolent power. In Brobdingnag, he is reduced to a squeaking insect, batted about by wasps and dropped into bowls of cream. His physical humiliations erode the complacency he brought from England, preparing the ground for the darker misanthropy of the later voyages. Even so, within Books I and II, Gulliver never fully connects his personal humbling to a larger critique of human vanity; he simply records the distress and soldiers on. That partial blindness makes him the perfect satiric conduit, because it forces the active reader to make the connections that the narrator cannot. Swift, through Gulliver, thus delivers a double blow: he shows a civilisation rotten to its core, and he simultaneously indicts the kind of mind—earnest, patriotic, incurious—that sustains such a civilisation by refusing to see it plain.
