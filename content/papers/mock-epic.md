Real heroism has disappeared; it has been replaced by fashionable entertainment. Pope’s aristocrats live in a world of leisure, gossip, and spectacle where card games are treated with greater seriousness than moral or intellectual concerns. The scene demonstrates Pope’s extraordinary ability to combine elegance with irony. Readers laugh at the exaggerated treatment of the game, but they also recognize the emptiness of the society being portrayed. The climax of Cantos I–III is, of course, the cutting of Belinda’s lock. Pope presents the Baron’s action as though it were an epic conquest or heroic achievement. Before attempting the deed, the Baron builds an altar and offers prayers, parodying the ritual preparations of epic warriors. When the lock is finally cut, Pope describes the moment in highly dramatic language: “The meeting points the sacred hair dissever / From the fair head, forever and forever!” The mock-tragic tone makes the event appear catastrophically important, though the actual loss is insignificant. Belinda’s emotional reaction resembles the grief of tragic heroines in epic and tragedy, yet the object of mourning is merely a curl of hair. Pope’s satire reaches its sharpest point here. Eighteenth-century aristocratic society, he suggests, lacks proportion and depth; it invests enormous emotional energy in superficial matters while ignoring genuine moral concerns. The lock itself becomes a symbol of vanity, beauty, and fragile social reputation. Thus, through Cantos I–III of The Rape of the Lock, Pope masterfully adapts the conventions of epic poetry in order to satirize the fashionable world of eighteenth-century England. Invocations, supernatural machinery, heroic battles, elevated diction, and ceremonial rituals are all used ironically to expose a society dominated by vanity, artificiality, and trivial pursuits. Yet Pope’s satire is never entirely bitter. His tone remains playful, witty, and elegant, reflecting both his amusement at and fascination with aristocratic culture. As critic Hazlitt remarked, Pope possesses “the utmost elegance of thought and expression.” In this poem, that elegance becomes a powerful weapon of satire. By turning a stolen lock of hair into an epic event, Pope reveals how far modern society has drifted from the true ideals of heroism and moral greatness. 2 DASGUPTA
