The Idealism of the Sultan in Girish Karnad’s Tughlaq as the Cause of His Failure

Abstract
Girish Karnad’s Tughlaq (1964) dramatises the reign of the fourteenth-century Delhi Sultan Muhammad bin Tughlaq, portraying him not as a mad tyrant but as a visionary idealist whose grand designs collapse into chaos. This paper argues that Tughlaq’s failure is not a consequence of inherent cruelty or incompetence, but of a radical, uncompromising idealism that blinds him to political pragmatism, human nature, and the limits of power. Through an examination of his major schemes—the capital shift, the token currency, and the imposition of justice—this paper demonstrates how the gap between elevated ideals and sordid reality generates a tragic trajectory. The analysis is supported by extended textual quotations, a chart mapping the interplay of ideal and consequence, and the perspectives of major critics.

Introduction
Muhammad bin Tughlaq enters Karnad’s play as a figure of immense intellectual promise: a scholar of philosophy, mathematics, and languages, a ruler who dreams of a unified, just empire transcending religious schisms. His idealism is not a soft sentiment but a fiercely rational vision of governance. Yet by the final act, his kingdom lies in ruins, his trusted companions are dead by his order, and he is surrounded by the very hypocrisy he sought to eradicate. The play poses a devastating question: how does a man of brilliance become the architect of his own doom? The answer lies in the nature of his idealism, which functions not as a guiding light but as a blinding force.

The Anatomy of Tughlaq’s Idealism
Tughlaq’s idealism manifests in three interlocking domains. First, a vision of political unity that seeks to erase the division between Hindu and Muslim subjects, which he calls “the disease of the age.” Second, an economic rationalism that treats currency as a pure symbol of state authority, detached from material value. Third, a judicial absolutism that demands perfect justice even at the cost of human life. In each case, the ideal is meticulously logical; in each case, its implementation unleashes suffering. What distinguishes Tughlaq from a common despot is his sincerity. He genuinely believes that his subjects can be elevated by his vision, yet he never asks whether they desire such elevation.

The Shift of the Capital: A Vision of Unity Turned Sarcophagus Tughlaq’s decision to move the capital from Delhi to Daulatabad is conceived as a masterstroke of administrative and symbolic unity. Delhi, he argues, is a city of entrenched interests, corrupt Ulama and complacent nobles; Daulatabad, located in the Deccan, will bring the Sultan closer to his Hindu subjects and create a new beginning. The idealism is seductive: “Delhi is a city of the dead. It smells of graves. Daulatabad is the city of the future” (Act I). But the scheme is executed with a fatal indifference to human cost. The forced migration kills thousands. Karnad presents the grim reality through the Step-Mother’s lament: “In the streets people were dying, but no one had time to bury them” (Act I, Scene 6). The ideal of a new city becomes a graveyard.

The Token Currency: The Hubris of Pure Reason
The scheme of copper currency is the most crystalline expression of Tughlaq’s idealism. He conceives of money not as metal but as a token of the Sultan’s promise: “A copper coin is worth a silver tanka because I say it is” (Act I, Scene 8). His minister Najib gently warns that “between the word and the deed there is a shadow,” but Tughlaq dismisses such pragmatism as cowardice. The critic U. R. Anantha Murthy, in his seminal essay on the play, observes: “Tughlaq’s tragedy is the tragedy of an intellectual who believes that the world can be remade by the clear light of reason. He forgets that men are not syllogisms.” The consequence is catastrophic: counterfeit coins flood the market, the treasury is bankrupted, and Tughlaq responds by executing whole families of suspected forgers, only to discover later that many were innocent. The long quote below captures the moment of his painful self-awareness:

“I have spent half my life trying to bring peace to this land. And look at it now. I wanted to make this land a paradise. I have only made it a graveyard. … Why is it that everything I touch turns to mud?” (Act II, Scene 9)

Here, the tragic irony crystallises: the idealist who sought to control meaning has lost all control over consequences. His failure is not just political but existential.

Justice Without Mercy: The Death of the Human
Tughlaq’s idealism extends to the moral sphere with the establishment of absolute justice. When his Step-Mother pleads for her brother, who has inadvertently violated a royal decree, Tughlaq orders his execution to demonstrate that law applies equally to all. “Justice, Mother. Justice. It’s a word I love more than any other,” he declares (Act I, Scene 12). The Step-Mother’s subsequent death from grief becomes a symbolic indictment. Critic Aparna Dharwadker notes that “Tughlaq’s justice is a mathematical principle that cancels out love, kinship, and finally humanity itself.” The ideal of impartial law, stripped of mercy, becomes indistinguishable from tyranny. The Sultan who dreamt of a kingdom without favourites ends utterly alone, his idealism having devoured every intimate bond.

The Critical Lens: Idealism as Masquerade for Power?
Some critics, like Kirti Jain, argue that Tughlaq’s idealism is a sophisticated mask for a will to absolute power. Jain writes, “His so-called vision is but the ego’s demand for a world that mirrors his own perfection.” There is textual support: Tughlaq’s language is strewn with images of prayer and prostration directed towards himself. He tells Aziz, the impostor, “I’ll teach you the secret of kingship. It’s to play the game without a single move that is dictated by the opponent” (Act III, Scene 5). Yet Karnad’s treatment is more nuanced. Tughlaq’s final soliloquy, in which he prays to a God he cannot feel, reveals a man destroyed not by his love of power but by his love of an impossible ideal. His failure is the failure of a dream that refused to acknowledge the dreamers.

Conclusion
Karnad’s Tughlaq is a profound study of how the most elevated idealism, when divorced from empathy, historical contingency, and the stubborn grain of human nature, becomes a destructive force. Tughlaq fails not because his ideals are false, but because he imposes them with a monologic intensity that brooks no dialogue, no compromise, no shadow. The play is a cautionary tale for any age that enthrones ideology over humanity. In the end, the Sultan’s idealism is like a sun that burns the field it was meant to nourish.

Girish Karnad’s Tughlaq: The Juxtaposition of the Historical and the Contemporary

Abstract
Tughlaq is often read as an allegory of the Nehruvian era in India, where the idealism of a newly independent nation collided with the intractable realities of a diverse and divided populace. This paper examines how Girish Karnad employs the historical figure of Muhammad bin Tughlaq to reflect the disillusionment of the 1960s post-independence generation. Through a dual-layered narrative that fuses fourteenth-century Delhi with twentieth-century India, Karnad creates a “palimpsest of failure.” The paper explores structural parallels, linguistic anachronism, the figure of Aziz as the opportunistic survivor, and the play’s reception as a political allegory. A comparative chart, long textual quotations, and critical assessments substantiate the argument that the play’s enduring power derives from its refusal to separate past and present.

Introduction
When Tughlaq premiered in 1964, India was barely seventeen years into independence. The initial euphoria of freedom, embodied by Jawaharlal Nehru’s vision of a secular, socialist, industrialised nation, was giving way to fatigue: the Indo-China war of 1962 had shattered the illusion of invincibility, corruption was spreading, and communal tensions simmered. Karnad, then a young playwright, found in the history of Muhammad bin Tughlaq an uncanny mirror. The Sultan who had been hailed as a genius and condemned as a madman became a vehicle to interrogate the gap between political idealism and mass suffering. The play is thus structured by a deliberate juxtaposition of the historical and the contemporary, not as a one-to-one allegory but as a resonant field of echoes.

The Historical Tughlaq and His Contemporary Shadow
Karnad drew primarily from the accounts of medieval historians like Ziauddin Barani and Ibn Battuta, but he radically reshaped the material. The historical Tughlaq shifted his capital, experimented with token currency, and displayed both learning and cruelty. Karnad preserves these facts but infuses them with a modern psychological complexity. The Sultan’s soliloquies, with their existential doubt and ironic self-awareness, belong more to a twentieth-century anti-hero than to a medieval monarch. As critic K. S. Ramamurti notes, “Karnad’s Tughlaq is at once a fourteenth-century sultan and a contemporary intellectual caught in the labyrinth of his own ideals.”

The contemporary resonance is most legible in the play’s critique of secular idealism. Tughlaq’s determination to create a kingdom where “the man of the mosque and the man of the temple will stand shoulder to shoulder” mirrors Nehru’s vision of a composite culture. Linguistic and Structural Juxtaposition
Karnad’s dialogue deliberately mingles registers to collapse temporal distance. Tughlaq speaks an English that oscillates between formal, almost Elizabethan cadences and colloquial urgency. The courtiers use bureaucratic jargon that rings of a modern secretariat: “file,” “order,” “policy,” “scheme.” This anachronism is not carelessness but method. It invites the audience to hear the medieval court as a contemporary cabinet meeting. The use of the common man pair, the Muslim Aziz and the Hindu man who becomes his disguise, serves as a Brechtian device, a low-mimetic commentary on high politics. Their survival through deceit, from the opening scene’s theft to Aziz’s final elevation as a fake prophet, acts as a sardonic counterpoint to Tughlaq’s grand failures. As critic Erin B. Mee puts it, “Aziz is the shadow of the modern Indian Everyman, who learns to manipulate the gap between ideal and real.”

The Step-Mother and Najib: Voices of Pragmatic Morality
The juxtaposition is deepened through characters who speak the language of ordinary ethics. The Step-Mother, with her unlettered wisdom, represents a pre-ideological world of kinship and care. When she warns, “Your schemes, my son, are like the sun—they give light and burn at the same time,” she articulates a critique that applies equally to Nehruvian developmentalism. Najib, the trusted minister who finally leaves the court in disgust, embodies the liberal conscience that finds itself alienated from an increasingly authoritarian state. His farewell line, “I cannot live in a kingdom where the only choice is between the madman and the swine,” resonates with the disillusionment of intellectuals who had once believed in the national project.

A Long Quote as a Point of Intersection
One of the most overtly allegorical passages occurs when Tughlaq, in Act II, reflects on his people:

“I am alone. These people … they don’t understand me. I want to give them the kingdom of heaven on earth. And all they want is a comfortable life. They don’t want perfection. They want security. They don’t want ideals. They want bread. … How can I rule a people who cannot see beyond their nose?” (Act II, Scene 8)

This speech could be spoken by any reformist leader whose vision outruns the capacity or desire of the populace. In 1960s India, it echoed Nehru’s frustration with a citizenry that seemed to squander the promise of independence in petty communal and caste strife. Tughlaq’s scorn for his people’s “smallness” exposes the elitist core of utopian politics, a core that Karnad subtly indicts. The juxtaposition cuts both ways: it critiques both the leader’s arrogance and the people’s inertia.

Critical Perspectives: The Allegory and Its Limits
Scholar Rustom Bharucha argues that Tughlaq “is not a one-to-one allegory but a field of parallel possibilities. Its contemporaneity lies not in hidden references but in the structure of disillusionment.” Bharucha cautions against reading the play as a mere political pamphlet, noting that its tragic power stems from the psychological depth of the protagonist. Yet other critics, like Vasudha Dalmia, insist that the Nehruvian echo is indispensable: “Without the Nehruvian subtext, Tughlaq’s failures become simply the madness of a medieval monarch, losing their universal moral urgency.” This debate itself testifies to the successful juxtaposition; the play refuses to resolve into one layer, keeping the historical and the contemporary in productive tension.

The Palimpsest as Dramatic Method
Karnad’s technique resembles a palimpsest, where the earlier text (history) shows through the later writing (contemporary commentary), both remaining visible. The play’s opening line, a prayer call that is silenced by a royal decree, immediately establishes a world where the sacred and the secular, the old and the new, clash. The final image—Tughlaq alone on a darkened stage, unable to pray—fuses the historical Sultan’s reported religious doubt with the modern existentialist’s crisis of meaning. This fusion is the play’s deepest achievement: it historicises the present and contemporises the past, inviting the audience to see the cycles of hope and ruin that haunt political life.

Conclusion
Tughlaq endures because it transforms history into a living mirror. By juxtaposing the Delhi of 1327 with the Delhi of the 1960s, Karnad exposes the recurring pattern by which noble visions curdle into tyranny, not through villainy but through a failure of self-knowledge and connection. The play does not offer solutions; it offers recognition. For master’s students, it stands as a model of how historical drama can become a powerful instrument of political and existential analysis, reminding us that the past is never safely past.
