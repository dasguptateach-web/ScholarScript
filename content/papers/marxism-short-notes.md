## 1. Gramsci – Rule vs Hegemony

Antonio Gramsci’s distinction between “rule” (domination) and “hegemony” is one of the most fertile concepts in twentieth-century Marxist thought, and it fundamentally reshaped how we understand power, culture and consent. In his Prison Notebooks, Gramsci, reflecting on why the socialist revolution Marx predicted had not occurred in the West, argued that class power is maintained not simply by the coercive apparatus of the state — the police, the army, the prisons — but by a dense network of cultural, moral and intellectual institutions that manufacture spontaneous consent. “Rule,” or dominio, refers to direct, forceful domination exercised by the state when consent breaks down. Hegemony, by contrast, is the “spontaneous consent” given by the masses to the general direction imposed on social life by the dominant group; it is a form of leadership in which the ruling class’s worldview becomes the “common sense” of the whole society. As Gramsci famously wrote, the state is only the “outer ditch” of the fortress; the real “trench systems” of civil society — schools, churches, the media, even literature — secure the moral and philosophical leadership of the bourgeoisie long before coercion is needed. The operation of hegemony is profoundly cultural. It permeates everyday life through what Gramsci called the “organic intellectuals” — thinkers and writers who arise organically from a class and give it an awareness of its own function. The bourgeoisie has been supremely successful in creating such intellectuals, who naturalise the existing order by disseminating values, norms and tastes that appear universal but are in fact class-bound. For example, the idea of individual merit, the sanctity of private property, or the division between “high” and “low” culture all function hegemonically. They are not forced upon people at gunpoint; they are internalised as what is “right,” “civilised,” or “realistic.” The critic Perry Anderson, in his influential essay “The Antinomies of Antonio Gramsci,” clarified that hegemony in Gramsci’s usage is a relation between classes that includes economic concessions, political alliances and ideological leadership, not merely the manipulation of ideas. It is a form of power that operates by saturating the whole social process and organising the masses’ “entire system of beliefs, values, and rituals.” For literary and cultural studies, the concept of hegemony has been revolutionary. Raymond Williams, in Marxism and Literature, developed the terms “dominant,” “residual” and “emergent” cultures to describe how hegemony is never static but must be continually reproduced and fought over. Literature, in this view, is a field of struggle where hegemonic meanings are affirmed, but also where counter-hegemonic consciousness can be articulated. The job of the Marxist critic is not simply to denounce ruling-class ideas but to analyse how they have been made to seem natural and to trace the fissures and contradictions through which alternative, popular consciousness presses. Hegemony thus provides a way of understanding the political work of culture without reducing it to crude propaganda. It explains why the oppressed often consent to their own oppression, but also why that consent is always fragile, requiring constant renewal — a process in which art, education and intellectual life are central battlegrounds. 2. Engelsian Marxist Criticism Engelsian Marxist criticism derives from the letters, prefaces and occasional writings of Friedrich Engels, who, more than Marx himself, directly addressed the relationship between literature and the social process. Engels’s two most famous letters to novelists — to Minna Kautsky in 1885 and to Margaret Harkness in 1888 — contain in embryo a sophisticated materialist aesthetics that avoids the reduction of art to mere political slogan. To Kautsky, Engels wrote that the writer need not offer a direct solution to social problems, but should let the “motives of action” arise naturally from the historical situation; the “social conflicts” should be embodied in the characters’ lives, not declaimed from the author’s lectern. To Harkness, he famously defined realism as the depiction of “typical characters under typical circumstances,” and praised Balzac, a politically reactionary writer, for giving a more truthful picture of French society than all the professional economists. The key principle here is that literary truth can exceed the author’s conscious ideology: a writer who faithfully observes the movement of history may undermine his own class prejudices.

This dialectical core was seized upon by later Marxist critics, most notably the Hungarian philosopher Georg Lukács. In Studies in European Realism, Lukács built an entire critical method out of Engels’s observation about Balzac, arguing that the great realists — Balzac, Tolstoy, Thomas Mann — grasped the underlying dynamic of historical change and created characters in whom the essential conflicts of an epoch were concentrated. For Lukács, the “typical” is not the average but the most deeply historically representative. Engelsian criticism, in this tradition, is fundamentally a criticism of form: it asks whether the formal organisation of a work reveals or conceals the movement of social contradiction. It opposes both the formalist flight from history and the propagandist reduction of art to politics. The critic Terry Eagleton, in Marxism and Literary Criticism, sums up the Engelsian position by noting that for Engels “art is most politically effective when it is least overtly political,” because its task is not to preach but to make us feel the historical forces that shape human destiny. The influence of Engelsian criticism persists in contemporary materialist analysis. It provides a defence of literary value that is not aestheticist; a work is “good” not because it is beautiful in the abstract, but because it reveals the real structures of social life with a concreteness that ideology normally obscures. The emphasis on “typical circumstances” has been refined by critics like Fredric Jameson, who in The Political Unconscious treats literary form as a “socially symbolic act” in which the antinomies of a class society are symbolically resolved. Engels’s insistence that political tendency should be hidden in the situation, not worn as a placard, also remains a powerful counter to any criticism that treats texts as straightforward party documents. It opens a space for the appreciation of works that are ideologically contradictory yet aesthetically powerful, reminding us that literature is not the passive reflection of an economic base but a creative, transformative practice that must be understood in all its formal complexity. 3. Leninist Marxist Criticism Leninist Marxist criticism is defined above all by the principle of partiinost (party-spiritedness or party commitment), most forcefully articulated in Lenin’s 1905 essay “Party Organisation and Party Literature.” Writing in the volatile moment of the first Russian Revolution, Lenin argued that in a class society there can be no such thing as non-partisan literature; all writing serves some class interest, however camouflaged. He called for literature that would become “a part of the general proletarian cause, a cog and a screw in the one single, great Social-Democratic mechanism.” At the same time, he insisted that literary work must not be mechanically standardised and that “the literary part of the proletarian cause cannot be stereotyped” — that the individual artist must be free to experiment and to write according to personal initiative. This dual insistence — literature must be committed, but literary work requires creative freedom — has been a source of enduring tension within Leninist criticism, opening a gap between the party’s demand for ideological correctness and the artist’s demand for autonomy. Lenin’s most substantial literary criticism is found in his essays on Leo Tolstoy, particularly “Tolstoy as the Mirror of the Russian Revolution” (1908). Here Lenin enacted a dialectical reading that is far more nuanced than the crude party prescription might suggest. He argued that Tolstoy’s greatness lay not in any conscious socialist programme — Tolstoy was a reactionary Christian anarchist — but in the way his art, in its very “contradictions,” reflected the mentality of millions of peasants during the revolutionary upheaval of 1905-07. Tolstoy “mirrored” the revolution not by endorsing it, but by embodying its deepest social contradictions: the naive egalitarianism and the patriarchal fatalism, the fierce protest against the state and the impotent withdrawal from politics. This method of “reflection” criticism, which reads literature as an index of class consciousness even when the author is ideologically backward, provided a model for subsequent Soviet criticism. It demonstrated that a Leninist critic could find profound value in a work that did not explicitly advance party politics, by tracing the real historical forces that structured its form and content. The institutionalisation of Leninism after the Bolshevik Revolution, particularly under Stalin, hardened partiinost into the state doctrine of Socialist Realism. At the First Congress of Soviet Writers in 1934, Andrei Zhdanov codified the demand that literature must be “tendentious,” depicting reality in its revolutionary development and educating the masses in the spirit of socialism. Avant-garde formalism and modernist experimentation were denounced as bourgeois decadence. Yet even within the Soviet system, Leninist criticism retained a more flexible dimension through thinkers like Mikhail Bakhtin (whose work on Rabelais and Dostoevsky ran against the current) and later Marxists like Galvano Della Volpe, who attempted to develop a

non-dogmatic materialist aesthetics. For better and worse, Leninist criticism made the social responsibility of literature inescapable; it forces every critic to confront the question of political alignment and to recognise that literary form is not a neutral playground but a field of class struggle. 4. Russian Formalism (Stalin) Russian Formalism, the brilliant school of literary scholarship pioneered by Viktor Shklovsky, Boris Eichenbaum, Roman Jakobson and Yury Tynyanov in the 1910s and 1920s, began with a revolutionary determination to make the study of literature a rigorous, autonomous science. Its founding principle, Shklovsky’s concept of ostranenie (defamiliarisation), held that art’s purpose is to renew perception by making the familiar strange — to “make the stone stony” again. The Formalists focused not on the author’s biography, the philosophical content, or the social background of the work, but on literariness — that which makes a verbal text a work of literature. They analysed the internal devices, the interplay of sounds and rhythms, the structural evolution of genres, with a precision that transformed literary studies. In the early Soviet period, this project was not necessarily anti-Marxist; it shared the general revolutionary mood of breaking with bourgeois academic sentimentalism. Trotsky, in Literature and Revolution (1924), engaged seriously with Formalism, acknowledging its scholarly ardour while criticising its neglect of the social content and ideological function of art. The relationship between Russian Formalism and the Stalinist state, however, became one of outright suppression. As Stalin consolidated power in the late 1920s, the cultural atmosphere shifted violently. The Bolshevik Party demanded that all intellectual work serve the goals of the five-year plans and the ideological mobilisation of the masses. Formalist concentration on verbal texture, sound patterns and narrative structure was branded “bourgeois aestheticism” and “anti-Marxist idealism.” The term “Formalism” itself became a term of political abuse. At the 1934 Soviet Writers’ Congress, where Socialist Realism was decreed the sole permissible literary method, Andrei Zhdanov explicitly attacked Formalist tendencies as alien to the people. Formalist critics were silenced; Shklovsky recanted in 1930, writing the self-denouncing article “A Monument to a Scholarly Error,” and the school dissolved under pressure. Jakobson had already emigrated; the others fell into obscurity, adapted by turning to historical or philological work, or perished in the purges. Victor Erlich’s classic study Russian Formalism: History, Doctrine details this tragic trajectory, noting that the Stalinist regime saw in the Formalists’ insistence on the autonomy of form a direct threat to the didactic, content-driven art it required. Nevertheless, the ideas of Russian Formalism survived underground and were later revived and transformed in Western structuralism and narratology. The Formalist legacy is a paradoxical one: it was crushed by Stalinism, yet its insistence on the specificity of the literary device has been incorporated, however uneasily, into sophisticated Marxist criticism. Fredric Jameson, in Marxism and Form, argued that Marxism needed Formalist tools to avoid the shallowness of vulgar content-analysis, for ideology is coded in form itself. The fate of Formalism under Stalin stands as a cautionary tale about the collision between intellectual inquiry and state-imposed orthodoxy, but also as a demonstration that ideas, once generated, cannot be permanently erased. 5. Marxist Literary Theory Marxist literary theory is not a single doctrine but a rich and internally contested tradition that applies the materialist conception of history to the analysis of literature. Its fundamental premise, drawn from Marx’s 1859 Preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy, is that the “mode of production of material life conditions the general process of social, political and intellectual life.” Literature, as part of the superstructure, arises from and responds to the economic base, but this relationship is never simple or mechanical. Early Marxist criticism often fell into a “reflection” model, reading literature as a direct transcript of class interest. But the tradition has developed enormously sophisticated accounts of mediation, relative autonomy and formal

complexity. From Engels’s emphasis on typicality, through Lukács’s theorisation of realism and modernism, to Gramsci’s concept of hegemony, the Frankfurt School’s critique of the culture industry, Althusser’s theory of ideology as the “lived relation” of men to their conditions of existence, and Jameson’s project of a “political unconscious,” Marxist theory insists that literature is a social practice that both reproduces and potentially transforms the ideological field in which it operates. The core concerns of Marxist literary theory can be grouped around three large questions. First, the question of determination: how does the social totality enter the texture of literary form? Althusserian critics such as Pierre Macherey, in A Theory of Literary Production, argued that ideology is not a content to be extracted but the “silences” and “absences” that structure the text, the gaps where the work’s own logic breaks down under the pressure of reality it cannot fully represent. Second, the question of function: what work does literature do in class society? This is where Gramsci’s hegemony becomes crucial, explaining how literary culture helps to manufacture consent for the existing order, while also being a site where counter-hegemonic consciousness can be forged. Third, the question of value: why do some works survive? For Lukács, the answer lies in their historical concreteness; for the later Eagleton, in Criticism and Ideology, aesthetic value is the form in which the contradictions of ideology are produced and made perceptible. Today, Marxist literary theory remains indispensable because it refuses the compartmentalisation of literature into a realm of pure, disinterested beauty. It connects the smallest stylistic detail — a plot shift, a point of view, a metaphor — to the vast movements of history and the struggles of social classes. It has absorbed the lessons of Formalism, structuralism, psychoanalysis and feminism without abandoning its core conviction that “it is not the consciousness of men that determines their being, but their social being that determines their consciousness.” As Jameson argued in The Political Unconscious, the task of Marxist criticism is to restore to the surface of the text the buried history of class struggle, exploitation and utopian desire that every literary work, however unwittingly, encodes. It is, in Eagleton’s words, “the most rigorous and comprehensive form of criticism yet developed,” precisely because it seeks to grasp literature not as an isolated artefact but as one moment in the total life of a society — and to change the conditions that make literature both necessary and, in its present form, a witness to injustice.
