## 1. Publication History

Sir Philip Sidney, Astrophel and Stella
Composed c. 1581–82 while Sidney was lodged at the home of Lady Penelope Rich. The sequence circulated in manuscript among courtly circles but was not printed until after Sidney’s death. The first, unauthorized quarto, published by Thomas Newman in 1591, created a sensation and launched the English sonnet vogue. “Loving in truth” is the opening sonnet, setting forth the poet-lover’s ars poetica.

Edmund Spenser, Amoretti
Published as part of Amoretti and Epithalamion by William Ponsonby in 1595. The sequence of 89 sonnets charts the poet’s courtship of Elizabeth Boyle, whom he eventually married. Sonnet 75, “One day I wrote her name upon the strand,” stands as the most famous lyric in the collection, dramatising the contest between mortality and art.

William Shakespeare, Shake-speares Sonnets
Published in 1609 by Thomas Thorpe in a quarto that remains our only authoritative text. The dedication to “Mr. W. H.” has never been conclusively explained. Sonnet 18, “Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?,” belongs to the “Fair Youth” sequence (sonnets 1–126) and is arguably the most celebrated lyric in the English language.

## 2. About the Poets

Sir Philip Sidney (1554–1586)
Courtier, soldier, scholar, and the very image of Renaissance chivalry. Educated at Shrewsbury School and Christ Church, Oxford, he travelled extensively on the Continent, meeting humanists and poets. His Defence of Poesy (c. 1579–80) is the first major work of English literary criticism. Sidney died heroically at the Battle of Zutphen, aged 31, cementing his legend. Astrophel and Stella is the first fully realised English sonnet sequence.

Edmund Spenser (c. 1552–1599)
Born in London and educated at Merchant Taylors’ School and Pembroke College, Cambridge. Spenser served as secretary to Lord Grey de Wilton in Ireland, where he wrote the bulk of The Faerie Queene (Books I–III, 1590). His eclogue collection The Shepheardes Calender (1579) announced a new age of English poetry. Amoretti is the only major Elizabethan sonnet sequence to end in marital union, and Spenser’s innovative sonnet form bears his name.

William Shakespeare (1564–1616)
Dramatist, actor, and poet from Stratford-upon-Avon. By the mid-1590s he had already written Venus and Adonis (1593) and The Rape of Lucrece (1594). His 154 sonnets, though published late, were likely composed across the 1590s and early 1600s. They represent the summit of the English sonnet tradition, pushing the Petrarchan conventions into profound psychological and philosophical territory.

## 3. Contributions to Elizabethan Poetry

Sidney: Fused Petrarchan love-melancholy with English rhetorical wit and self-conscious artistry. The Defence of Poesy exalts the poet as a maker who creates a golden world beyond nature. Astrophel and Stella demonstrates that the vernacular could rival Italian and French lyricism.

Spenser: Blended medieval allegory, classical epic, and Protestant morality. In The Shepheardes Calender he revived the eclogue; in The Faerie Queene he invented the Spenserian stanza for epic romance. Amoretti — and the linked Epithalamion — transform the Petrarchan sequence into a narrative of faithful, mutual, Christian love culminating in marriage.

Shakespeare: Reinvented the sonnet through emotional immediacy, dramatic argument, and linguistic density. His speaker addresses a young man rather than an inaccessible mistress, subverting gender conventions. Shakespearean sonnets are driven by a powerful “turn” or volta, often delayed to the final couplet, yielding epigrammatic closure.

## 4. Sonnet Forms

## Petrarchan (Italian)

## Structure: Octave + sestet (8 + 6 lines)

Rhyme scheme: Usually abbaabba for the octave; sestet varies: cdecde, cdcdcd, cdeced, etc.

Volta: Typically occurs at line 9, signalling a turn of thought.

Function: The octave presents a problem, situation, or question; the sestet resolves, answers, or meditates upon it.

Presence in the three poems: Sidney’s sonnet exhibits a blend of Italian and English patterns; neither Spenser nor Shakespeare wrote pure Petrarchan sonnets, but the Petrarchan logic of argument and counter-argument underpins all three.

## Shakespearean (English)

Structure: Three quatrains + a final rhymed couplet (4+4+4+2)

## Rhyme scheme: abab cdcd efef gg

Volta: Can appear at line 9, but often delayed to the couplet or even to the twelfth line, giving a powerful final reversal or summation.

Example: Sonnet 18 (Shall I compare thee...) follows this form exactly.

## Spenserian

Structure: Three quatrains + a couplet, but with interlocking rhymes that weave the quatrains together.

## Rhyme scheme: abab bcbc cdcd ee

Volta: Typically occurs at line 13 (the couplet), though a nuanced turn may appear earlier.

Effect: The linked rhymes propel the reader forward, mirroring Spenser’s narrative of progressive, unfolding love.

Example: Amoretti 75 uses the Spenserian rhyme chain.

## Sidney’s Hybrid in Astrophel and Stella 1

## Rhyme scheme: abab abab cdcd ee

Structure: Two quatrains sharing identical end-sounds (abab repeated) give a sense of obsessive, circular striving; a third quatrain with a new rhyme (cdcd) introduces a shift; the couplet clinches the poem.

Volta: The turn from frustrated invention to inspiration occurs abruptly at the couplet, dramatically set off by the direct speech of the Muse.

## 5. Texts of the Poems

Sir Philip Sidney – Loving in Truth (Astrophel and Stella, Sonnet 1)

Loving in truth, and fain in verse my love to show,
That she, dear she, might take some pleasure of my pain;
Pleasure might cause her read, reading might make her know;
Knowledge might pity win, and pity grace obtain.
I sought fit words to paint the blackest face of woe,
Studying inventions fine, her wits to entertain;
Oft turning others’ leaves, to see if thence would flow
Some fresh and fruitful showers upon my sunburned brain.
But words came halting forth, wanting Invention’s stay;
Invention, Nature’s child, fled step-dame Study’s blows;
And others’ feet still seemed but strangers in my way.
Thus great with child to speak, and helpless in my throes,
Biting my truant pen, beating myself for spite,
“Fool,” said my Muse to me, “look in thy heart and write.”

Edmund Spenser – One Day I Wrote Her Name (Amoretti, Sonnet 75)

One day I wrote her name upon the strand,
But came the waves and washed it away:
Again I wrote it with a second hand,
But came the tide, and made my pains his prey.
“Vain man,” said she, “that dost in vain assay,
A mortal thing so to immortalize,
For I myself shall like to this decay,
And eke my name be wiped out likewise.”
“Not so,” (quod I) “let baser things devise
To die in dust, but you shall live by fame:
My verse your virtues rare shall eternize,
And in the heavens write your glorious name.
Where whenas death shall all the world subdue,
Our love shall live, and later life renew.”

William Shakespeare – Shall I Compare Thee (Sonnet 18)

Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate:
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer’s lease hath all too short a date;
Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimm’d;
And every fair from fair sometime declines,
By chance or nature’s changing course untrimm’d;
But thy eternal summer shall not fade,
Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow’st;
Nor shall death brag thou wander’st in his shade,
When in eternal lines to time thou grow’st:
So long as men can breathe or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.

## 8. Rhetorical Devices

## Sidney – Loving in Truth

Gradatio / Climax: “Pleasure might cause her read, reading might make her know; / Knowledge might pity win, and pity grace obtain” — a staircase effect linking desire to ultimate reward.

Personification: “Invention, Nature’s child,” “step-dame Study’s blows”; “Muse” speaking.

Metaphor: “fresh and fruitful showers” → inspiration; “sunburned brain” → intellectual sterility; “great with child” → creative pregnancy.

Paradox: “pleasure of my pain”; “words came halting forth” — the failure of verse performed in perfectly fluent verse.

Pun / Syllepsis: “feet” = metrical units and strangers’ footprints.

Prosopopoeia: The Muse speaks directly, shattering the introspective agony.

## Antithesis: “Nature’s child” vs “step-dame Study.”

## Spenser – One Day I Wrote Her Name

Dialogue / Dialectic: The poem divides into the beloved’s speech (lines 5–8) and the poet’s reply (9–14), echoing a Renaissance dispute (disputatio).

Personification: “waves… washed it away”; “tide… made my pains his prey”; Death “shall all the world subdue.”

Anaphora: “But came the waves… But came the tide…” (lines 2 and 4) emphasises relentless erosion.

Chiasmus / Reversal: “Vain man… in vain assay” — double “vain” mirrors the futility.

Apostrophe: The poet addresses the beloved (absent in the dramatic replay).

Hyperbole: “in the heavens write Find your glorious name” — cosmic inscription.

Epizeuxis / Repetition: “die in dust” alliterates and amplifies finality; “Our love shall live, and later life renew” — “life” renew resonates with resurrection.

Paronomasia: “strand” (beach / thread of a narrative).

## Shakespeare – Shall I Compare Thee…

Rhetorical Question: Opening line — “Shall I compare thee…?” sets up the entire argument.

Anaphora: “And summer’s lease… And often is his gold complexion…” (lines 4,6); “Nor lose… Nor shall death…” (lines 10–11) builds insistent negation.

Personification: Summer (lease-holder), sun (eye of heaven), Death (braggart).

Metaphor: “eye of heaven” for sun; “eternal lines” for poetry; “shade” for death’s domain.

Polyptoton: “fair from fair” — wordplay on the noun and adjective.

Antithesis: Temporal summer vs. “eternal summer”; death’s boast vs. poetic immortality.

Paradox: The beloved “grow’st” in “eternal lines” — growing inside something that is itself unchanging.

Couplet Epigram: The final two lines deliver a perfectly balanced, lapidary conclusion.

## 9. Prosody: Scansion and Sound

## Sidney

Meter: Predominantly iambic pentameter, but with notable variations.

Line 1: “LOving in TRUTH, and FAIN in VERSE my LOVE to SHOW” — a spondee (“LOving in TRUTH” perhaps) on the opening creates earnest emphasis.

Line 9: “But WORDS came HALting FORTH, WANTing InVENtion’s STAY” — the caesura after “forth” and the halting rhythm mirror the limping words.

Rhyme scheme: abab abab cdcd ee. The repeated abab in the second quatrain conveys an obsessive revisiting of the same sonic ground; the shift to cdcd marks a change, and the couplet ee hammers home the Muse’s dictum.

Enjambment: Very limited; most lines are end-stopped, giving the poem a controlled, syntactically segmented feel that reflects the poet’s failed effort at invention — until the Muse’s speech, which breaks the pattern with its direct imperative.

Sound: Alliteration of “p” in “pleasure… pain” (line 2) and “b” in “biting… beating” (line 13); sibilance in “showers upon my sunburned” echoes the longed-for showers.

## Spenser

Meter: Iambic pentameter throughout, regular yet gracefully varied.

“One DAY I WROTE her NAME upON the STRAND” — initial iamb, but “One day I wrote” starts with a stress (trochaic substitution) lending narrative immediacy.

“But CAME the TIDE, and MADE my PAINS his PREY” — the heavily stressed “CAME,” “MADE,” “PAINS,” “PREY” create a surging rhythm of assault.

Rhyme scheme: abab bcbc cdcd ee (Spenserian). The interlocking rhymes link each quatrain to the next, mimicking the in-rushing tide and the flowing dialogue.

Volta: Arrives with “Not so” (line 9), sharply breaking the beloved’s speech; the rhyme shifts from her *-ay*/-ize pattern into his triumphant -ize/-ame chain.

Caesura: Strong medial pause in line 5: ““Vain man,” said she, “that dost in vain assay” — the pause dramatises her incredulity.

Alliteration: “waves… washed… wrote” (w); “die in dust” (d); “love shall live… later life” (l) — the liquid sounds of “l” lift the final couplet toward transcendence.

## Shakespeare

**Meter: Iambic pentameter with masterly variations.**

Opening: “Shall I comPARE thee TO a SUMmer’s DAY?” — a perfect iambic line; the regularity makes the subsequent disruption in “ROUGH WINDS do SHAKE…” palpable.

“And EVery FAIR from FAIR someTIME deCLINES” — the spondaic “EVERy FAIR from FAIR” slows the pace, enacting the process of decline.

Final couplet: “So LONG as MEN can BREATHE or EYES can SEE, / So LONG lives THIS, and THIS gives LIFE to THEE” — emphatic spondees on “So LONG” and “THIS, and THIS” give monumental finality.

Rhyme scheme: abab cdcd efef gg. Each quatrain introduces a fresh rhyme pair, propelling the argument forward; the final couplet provides an epigrammatic seal.

Volta: Line 9, “But thy eternal summer shall not fade” — a decisive logical pivot underscored by the coordinating conjunction “But.”

Assonance/Consonance: “shake… darling buds… May” (long a); “too hot… heaven… gold” (h and long o interplay); “breathe… see… thee” (ee) yields an echoing eternal note.

| Line | Text | Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Loving in truth, and fain in verse my love to show | The poet asserts his sincerity (“in truth”) and his desire (“fain”) to manifest love in poetry. The conjunction of emotional truth with literary ambition sets up the central tension. |
| 2 | That she, dear she, might take some pleasure of my pain | The beloved (“dear she”) is the reader he hopes to please. The paradox “pleasure of my pain” encapsulates the Petrarchan love-suffering trope. |
| 3–4 | Pleasure might cause her read, reading might make her know; / Knowledge might pity win, and pity grace obtain | A rhetorical gradatio (climax): pleasure → reading → knowledge → pity → grace. The desired chain of effects shows that poetry is instrumental—a means to win her favour. |
| 5–6 | I sought fit words to paint the blackest face of woe, / Studying inventions fine, her wits to entertain | He adopts the stance of a studious poet, seeking “fit words” to depict extreme sorrow. “Inventions fine” suggests both rhetorical ornaments and intellectual conceits. |
| 7–8 | Oft turning others’ leaves, to see if thence would flow / Some fresh and fruitful showers upon my sunburned brain | He reads other poets (“others’ leaves”) for inspiration. The metaphor of “fresh and fruitful showers” on his “sunburned brain” pictures his intellect as parched and infertile. |
| 9–10 | But words came halting forth, wanting Invention’s stay; / Invention, Nature’s child, fled step-dame Study’s blows | The failure: words limp, lacking the support (“stay”) of true creative invention. Personification: Invention is “Nature’s child,” opposed to the cruel stepmother “Study.” Artifice has driven away authentic creativity. |
| 11 | And others’ feet still seemed but strangers in my way | “Feet” puns on metrical feet; the poetic forms of others feel alien, blocking his own expression. |
| 12 | Thus great with child to speak, and helpless in my throes | A startling male-pregnancy metaphor: he is labouring to give birth to verse but is helpless. Conveys creative agony. |
| 13 | Biting my truant pen, beating myself for spite | Physical gestures of frustration and self-punishment; “truant” pen implies it has neglected its duty. |
| 14 | “Fool,” said my Muse to me, “look in thy heart and write.” | The volta: the Muse breaks in. The solution is not imitation but introspection. The final line is one of the most famous injunctions in English poetry—sincerity of feeling outweighs all learned ornament. |

| Line | Text | Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | One day I wrote her name upon the strand | Setting: a seaside. Writing the beloved’s name in sand is a gesture of devotion but also an emblem of ephemerality. |
| 2 | But came the waves and washed it away | Nature’s agency undoes human inscription instantly. |
| 3–4 | Again I wrote it with a second hand, / But came the tide, and made my pains his prey | Persistence: a second attempt, but the tide personified as a predator (“his prey”) devours his effort. The repetition enacts the cyclical struggle. |
| 5–6 | “Vain man,” said she, “that dost in vain assay, / A mortal thing so to immortalize” | The beloved speaks: she calls him “vain” (foolish, prideful) for attempting to immortalise something inherently mortal. “Assay” means attempt; the double “vain” underscores futility. |
| 7–8 | “For I myself shall like to this decay, / And eke my name be wiped out likewise” | She reasons that just as the writing vanishes, so too will her physical self and her earthly name. Eke = also. |
| 9–10 | “Not so,” (quod I) “let baser things devise / To die in dust, but you shall live by fame” | The volta: the poet refutes her. “Quod” = said. Lower, material things may perish, but she will achieve immortal fame. |
| 11–12 | “My verse your virtues rare shall eternize, / And in the heavens write your glorious name” | His poetry will confer eternity; note the shift from “strand” to “heavens” — from mutable earth to transcendent permanence. “Eternize” is a characteristically Spenserian coinage. |
| 13–14 | “Where whenas death shall all the world subdue, / Our love shall live, and later life renew” | The couplet expands the victory: even after the eschaton (“whenas death shall all the world subdue”), their love will participate in the resurrection and renewal of life. This is a triumphant Christian resolution unique in Elizabethan love sonnets. |

| Line | Text | Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day? | A rhetorical question that proposes a conventional Petrarchan comparison — but the poem will subvert it. |
| 2 | Thou art more lovely and more temperate | Immediate answer: the beloved surpasses summer. “Temperate” suggests steadiness and moderation vs. summer’s extremes. |
| 3–4 | Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May, / And summer’s lease hath all too short a date | Summer’s flaws: destructive winds, short duration. The legal/financial metaphor “lease” and “date” imply a contract with a terminal limit. |
| 5–6 | Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines, / And often is his gold complexion dimm’d | The sun (“eye of heaven”) is excessive or obscured; “gold complexion” suggests both brightness and regal beauty, but it is inconstant. |
| 7–8 | And every fair from fair sometime declines, / By chance or nature’s changing course untrimm’d | Universal law: all beautiful things lose their beauty, stripped (“untrimm’d”) by accident or natural mutability. “Fair from fair” is a polyptoton (repetition of a root word in different forms). |
| 9 | But thy eternal summer shall not fade | The turn: the beloved’s “eternal summer” contrasts with the literal summer. Volta pivots on “But.” |
| 10 | Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow’st | He will not forfeit (“lose possession”) his beauty; “ow’st” means both ownest and owest (as a debt), playing on possession and obligation. |
| 11 | Nor shall death brag thou wander’st in his shade | Death is personified as a braggart; the poet denies him victory. “Shade” = the underworld’s darkness. |
| 12 | When in eternal lines to time thou grow’st | “Lines” may denote both poetic lines and lineage (family lines). The beloved’s growth is into and against Time itself; he is grafted onto eternity through verse. |
| 13–14 | So long as men can breathe or eyes can see, / So long lives this, and this gives life to thee | Couplet: the poem’s existence and its effects are coterminous with humanity’s sensory life. “This” = the sonnet; the beloved’s immortality is purely textual, yet absolute. |
