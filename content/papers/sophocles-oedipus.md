## 1. Role of Teiresias

The entry of the blind prophet Teiresias into Oedipus Rex is the hinge upon which the entire tragedy swings. Before his arrival, Oedipus is the confident, beloved saviour-king, the master of riddles who pledged to root out the pollution that causes the Theban plague. The moment Teiresias speaks — or rather, refuses to speak, and then is goaded into speech — the drama transforms from a public investigation into a private nightmare. Teiresias functions simultaneously as a character, a symbol, and an instrument of the divine plot. He is the physical embodiment of the paradox of sight and blindness that runs through the play; he is the reluctant revealer of a truth that no one wishes to hear; and he is the primary catalyst of Oedipus’s tragic self-discovery, forcing the king onto the path that leads to peripeteia and anagnorisis. His role is so pivotal that without him the mechanism of fate would remain a secret, and the tragedy would have no engine. Teiresias enters at line 300 of the Greek text, summoned by Oedipus on the advice of Creon, who has returned from Delphi with Apollo’s command to expel the murderer of Laius. Oedipus greets him with elaborate respect: “Teiresias, master of all mysteries of life — what can be known and what cannot — you know the secrets of heaven and earth; you know the city’s plight.” The king’s rhetoric sets up a dramatic irony that will immediately collapse. Oedipus assumes that the prophet, like himself, will use his knowledge to save the city. But Teiresias knows that the truth will destroy Oedipus and devastate the royal house. His first words are a cry of pain: “Ah! How terrible it is to know when knowledge brings no benefit to the knower! I knew this well, but I forgot; otherwise I would never have come.” This self-reproach establishes Teiresias as a deeply human figure beneath the vatic mantle. He is not a cold supernatural agent but a man trapped between divine knowledge and human compassion. His initial refusal to speak — “Let me go home; it will be easiest for you and me if you let me follow my own path” — is an act of pity, perhaps the only pure gesture of mercy in the entire play. Oedipus, however, interprets this silence as treason. Here the role of Teiresias shifts from reluctant prophet to the mirror of Oedipus’s tyrannical temper. The king’s rage at being denied the information he demanded escalates with frightening speed: Teiresias is a “wicked old man,” a “plotter” in league with Creon, a “stone-blind, stone-deaf fool.” The irony is, as the critic Bernard Knox has meticulously shown in Oedipus at Thebes, that every insult Oedipus hurls at Teiresias is true of himself. Teiresias is blind physically, but Oedipus is blind intellectually and morally. Teiresias is accused of being “blind in mind and ears as well as in your eyes,” yet it is Oedipus who cannot hear the truth even when it is shouted at him. The prophet, pushed beyond endurance, finally speaks the truth: “I say that you are the murderer whom you seek… You are living in shame with your nearest and dearest, unaware of the depth of your own degradation.” These lines are the unambiguous exposure of the secret that will dominate the rest of the play. Teiresias’s role, therefore, is to utter the truth once and for all, so that the audience, the Chorus, and even Oedipus at some buried level have heard it, even if they cannot yet accept it. Teiresias then delivers a riddling, terrifying speech that transforms him from a mere witness into a prophet of doom. He describes the murderer of Laius as “a man who will be revealed as a native-born Theban, but who will find no joy in that revelation; a blind man who once had sight, a beggar who was once rich, he will feel his way with a stick towards a foreign land.” This prophecy not only names Oedipus’s future but mirrors the play’s entire future action in advance. Teiresias’s role here is akin to that of the oracle at Delphi: he utters the divine design in a form that is perfectly clear in retrospect but baffling in the present. The audience, knowing the myth, shudders at the accuracy; Oedipus, the character, hears only a riddle, the sort of riddle he once solved to defeat the Sphinx. It is a masterstroke of Sophoclean irony that Oedipus, the great interpreter of riddles, cannot interpret the riddle of his own life. The critic E. R. Dodds, in his landmark essay “On Misunderstanding the Oedipus Rex,” emphasises that Teiresias’s speech is not a piece of dramatic decoration but the voice of the god Apollo himself. Through Teiresias, Apollo speaks directly into the action, making the divine will known even as it is resisted. The confrontation between Oedipus and Teiresias also serves to flesh out the play’s central thematic opposition: sight versus blindness, knowledge versus ignorance. Teiresias’s physical blindness, juxtaposed with his inner vision, is the antithesis to Oedipus’s physical sight, which is paired with his inner blindness. The prophet articulates this with a devastating line: “You mock my blindness, do you? But I say to you, you with your precious eyes, you are blind to the corruption of your own life.” This antithesis will become literalised in the final act when Oedipus puts out his own eyes. Teiresias’s role is to prefigure that climax, to make the audience feel the eventual self-blinding as the physical manifestation of an inner truth that has been true all

along. As the classicist Charles Segal argues in Oedipus Tyrannus: Tragic Heroism and the Limits of Knowledge, Teiresias is the play’s keeper of the boundary between human and divine knowledge. He shows that the most profound seeing is done not with the eyes but with the mind that has been opened by the gods — and that the cost of such vision is immense suffering. Teiresias’s final lines before he exits are a reiteration of the riddle and a withdrawal: “The man you have been trying to find with your threats and your proclamations about the death of Laius — that man is here. He is considered a foreign immigrant, but he will be found to be a native Theban, and he will find no pleasure in that discovery.” He then moves towards the doors, calling for a guide, and vanishes from the play. His exit is as abrupt as his truth. He does not remain to explain, to console, or to witness the destruction. His role is not to be a participant in the action but a trigger; once the truth has been released into the play, Teiresias is no longer needed. The truth will do its own work. The critic R. P. Winnington-Ingram, in Sophocles: An Interpretation, compares Teiresias to a midwife who delivers the truth and then departs, leaving others to deal with the consequences. This is an essential aspect of his role: he is not the agent of punishment, not the judge, merely the mouthpiece of what is and what must be. Finally, Teiresias functions as a test of Oedipus’s character. The king’s response to the prophet’s truth-telling reveals the tyrannical paranoia that lurks beneath the benevolent ruler. Before Teiresias enters, Oedipus has shown himself to be compassionate, decisive, a true father to his people. After Teiresias speaks, we see the Oedipus who will kill a stranger at a crossroads, who will torture a shepherd, who will immediately suspect his loyal brother-in-law of treason without a shred of evidence. Teiresias’s role is thus to strip the mask of the enlightened monarch and expose the fearful, violent, self-protective man beneath. This is not to condemn Oedipus; it is to make him tragic. He is not a villain but a human being whose very virtues — his determination, his trust in his own intelligence — become the instruments of his ruin. Teiresias, by showing Oedipus what he refuses to see, sets the tragic machinery in motion and defines the terms of the play’s ultimate catastrophe. 2. Discuss the Role of Fate in Oedipus the King (Peripeteia and Anagnorisis) Fate in Oedipus Rex is not a capricious doom inflicted by malicious gods upon an innocent man; it is a complex, interlocking structure of divine foreknowledge and human action that produces the most perfectly constructed tragic plot in the Western canon. Sophocles dramatises fate not as a passive destiny that Oedipus helplessly endures, but as a prophecy that his every attempt to escape serves to fulfil. The role of fate is to provide the inescapable framework within which the drama’s two essential moments — peripeteia (reversal of fortune) and anagnorisis (recognition) — occur. These two terms, drawn from Aristotle’s Poetics, name the pivot points where the king’s prosperity collapses into ruin and his ignorance collapses into knowledge, and both are the direct result of the oracular fate that governs the house of Laius. The fate of Oedipus is set by the oracle of Apollo at Delphi. Before his birth, his father Laius was told that any son born of Jocasta would kill his father and marry his mother. When Oedipus is born, Laius and Jocasta pierce his ankles and expose him on Mount Cithaeron, seeking to murder the child and thus cancel the prophecy. This act of attempted infanticide is the first link in the chain of causation that constitutes the play’s notion of fate. As the critic E. R. Dodds insisted in “On Misunderstanding the Oedipus Rex,” Sophocles’ audience would not have seen fate as an external force overriding human will; they would have seen a divine law that operates through and because of human decisions. Laius and Jocasta chose to kill their child; the shepherd chose to save him; Polybus and Merope chose to adopt him; Oedipus chose to flee Corinth upon hearing a similar oracle. Every link in the chain is a human choice, and yet the total pattern realises the divine word perfectly. Fate is the pattern, not the push. Peripeteia — the sudden reversal of fortune from good to bad — occurs with the arrival of the Corinthian Messenger, a scene Aristotle praises as a model of tragic plotting. The Messenger comes to announce the death of Polybus and to offer Oedipus the throne of Corinth. For a brief, brilliant moment, Jocasta exults: “Where are you now, divine oracles? The man Oedipus feared he would kill is dead, and his death was natural.” The news seems to prove that the oracle was false, that Oedipus has outrun his fate. But the Messenger’s casual revelation that Oedipus was not the natural son of Polybus — “Polybus was no more related to you than I am” — instantly transforms the good news into the worst possible news. This is the peripeteia: the very piece of information

that appears to liberate Oedipus from his fear of the oracle becomes the clue that proves the oracle has already been fulfilled. Fortune reverses in a heartbeat, and the reversal is produced not by a new event but by a new understanding of an old event. Aristotle notes in the Poetics that the best peripeteia occurs when an action designed to produce one result produces its opposite, and Sophocles’ construction here is the textbook example. The Messenger brings a message of joy, and it creates the conditions for horror. The anagnorisis, or recognition, follows with irresistible momentum from the peripeteia. Anagnorisis is the passage from ignorance to knowledge, and in Oedipus Rex it unfolds in stages, each peeling back a layer of Oedipus’s identity until the naked truth stands exposed. The process begins with the Messenger’s revelation of Oedipus’s foundling origin and continues with the arrival of the Theban Shepherd, the very man who had been ordered to expose the infant on the mountain. Oedipus, now in a frenzy of desperate need to know, forces the Shepherd to speak, although the old man tries repeatedly to stop: “I am on the verge of saying something terrible.” The final recognition — that Oedipus is the son of Laius and Jocasta, the killer of his father, the husband of his mother — lands with the force of a physical blow. Oedipus cries out: “Oh! Oh! All is clear! Let it be now, light, let me see you for the last time! I stand revealed — born in shame, married in shame, a murderer through my marriage.” This is the moment of anagnorisis, when the scattered pieces of the puzzle snap into a single, devastating image. What makes this recognition so powerful, and so bound up with the concept of fate, is that it is entirely self-inflicted. No god descends to reveal the truth; no Teiresias spells it out in plain language. Oedipus pursues the truth with the same relentless intelligence with which he once solved the Sphinx’s riddle. Fate does not force him to interrogate the Shepherd; he does it himself, overriding Jocasta’s desperate plea: “Stop! For the gods’ sake, if you care for your own life, stop this search! My suffering is enough.” Oedipus’s own character — his intellectual pride, his sense of responsibility, his refusal to live in comfortable ignorance — makes him the agent of his own recognition. As Bernard Knox argues in The Heroic Temper, Oedipus’s greatness lies precisely in this: he will not turn back, even when the truth becomes lethal. Fate sets the trap; Oedipus springs it through the exercise of his defining virtue. The relationship between fate and free will in the play has been a perennial debate. The critic Dodds firmly rejected the idea that Oedipus Rex is a “tragedy of fate” in the sense that Oedipus is a mere puppet. He points out that everything Oedipus does follows from his character: his decision to kill Laius at the crossroads was an act of anger, not necessity; his decision to marry Jocasta was an act of political ambition and perhaps sexual desire, not compulsion; his decision to hunt the murderer was an act of kingly responsibility. The oracle did not say, “You will be compelled to kill your father and marry your mother”; it said, “You will kill your father and marry your mother.” The futurity of the statement does not remove the voluntariness of the acts. Fate, in Sophocles’ handling, is the shape that human freedom takes when seen from the perspective of the gods. Nevertheless, the feeling of inescapability is essential to the tragedy’s emotional effect. The play generates a suffocating sense that every road Oedipus takes leads to the same destination. When he fled Corinth to avoid killing Polybus, he was walking straight towards Laius. When he solved the Sphinx’s riddle and won the throne of Thebes, he was walking straight into his mother’s bed. The oracle is like a net whose cords are the hero’s own limbs; every movement tightens it. This paradox — that the man most determined to control his own destiny is the one most thoroughly enmeshed in the destiny he seeks to avoid — is the play’s profoundest articulation of the role of fate. Peripeteia and anagnorisis are the dramatic crystallisations of this paradox: the moment when the net snaps shut, and the moment when the captured man sees the net. 3. The Character of Creon Creon in Oedipus Rex is a figure of remarkable restraint and political skill, whose function in the drama is to serve as a foil to Oedipus’s impetuous grandeur and to prefigure, through his moderation, the stability that will be required after the royal house collapses. He is not yet the tyrannical Creon of Antigone; in this play, he is the loyal brother-in-law, the careful politician, the man who does not desire supreme power and who therefore, in the Sophoclean moral universe, becomes the rightful recipient of it. His character is delineated with a subtlety that rewards close attention, for he operates as a mirror in which Oedipus’s flaws — rashness, suspicion, intellectual pride — are reflected and measured.

Creon enters the play as a returning emissary from Delphi, bearing Apollo’s command that the city must expel the murderer of Laius. His first speech is a model of diplomatic tact. When Oedipus asks him to report the oracle, Creon offers to speak privately, inside the palace: “If you want my report in the presence of these people, I am ready to speak; or let us go inside.” This small gesture reveals a great deal about Creon. He is sensitive to the public mood, aware that the oracle’s content might be politically explosive, and careful to defer to the king’s judgment. He does not assume authority or rush to publicise divine messages; he offers discretion. When Oedipus chooses the public forum, Creon obeys without demur. This pattern — loyalty, deference, absence of personal ambition — defines his behaviour throughout the play. The most dramatic test of Creon’s character comes when Oedipus, in his paranoid fury, accuses him of conspiring with Teiresias to seize the throne. The king levels a charge that is utterly baseless: Creon, he claims, has put Teiresias up to calling Oedipus the murderer in order to depose him. Creon’s response is one of the great speeches of self-defence in Greek tragedy, and it displays a rational, well-ordered mind. He argues first from probability: “Who would choose to be king in constant fear rather than sleep peacefully with the same power but fewer dangers?” He points out that as the brother of the queen, he already enjoys all the privileges of royalty without the anxiety of the top position. Then he proposes a logical test: “Go to Delphi yourself; ask the oracle if I reported the message faithfully. If you find I have plotted with the prophet, then put me to death — and I will vote with you for the sentence.” This is the language of a man who trusts in evidence, process, and reason. The critic H. D. F. Kitto, in Greek Tragedy, describes Creon at this moment as “the very voice of sanity in a world that is beginning to go mad.” His calm rebuttal makes Oedipus’s accusations look not only unjust but absurd. Yet Creon is not a mere plaster saint of rationality. He is capable of sharp, wounded dignity. When Oedipus refuses to listen to his defence and insists that Creon must die, Creon retorts: “Not unless you can prove I am guilty.” When Oedipus says, “I must have my way,” Creon replies, “Then you are wrong.” These exchanges reveal that Creon is not servile; he has a sense of his own honour and will not bend it even before an enraged king. The difference between him and Oedipus is that Creon’s self-assertion is bounded by a respect for justice, whereas Oedipus’s self-assertion has become indistinguishable from tyranny. The Chorus, the voice of the Theban elders, intervenes to save Creon, and Oedipus releases him with the bitter words: “Let him go then, even if it means my death or exile.” Creon leaves the stage with his dignity intact, having exposed the king’s temper without ever losing his own composure. This scene is critical for the audience’s judgment: Creon emerges as the man who can govern his passions, while Oedipus is governed by his. Creon returns at the end of the play, after the catastrophe has unfolded. The blinded Oedipus appears from the palace, and the power structure of Thebes shifts. With Jocasta dead and Oedipus self-condemned to exile, Creon becomes the de facto ruler. His behaviour in this final scene has been the subject of much critical discussion. Some readers, projecting backwards from the Antigone, detect a nascent authoritarianism in Creon’s assumption of control. But a careful reading of the text suggests otherwise. Creon enters not with triumph but with compassion. He does not gloat. He says to the attendants: “I have not come to mock you, Oedipus, nor to reproach you for your past misdeeds.” He orders the children — Antigone and Ismene — to be brought to their father, granting the blinded man the comfort he most craves. When Oedipus begs to be exiled immediately, Creon responds with characteristic prudence: “I would have done so, but I wanted first to ask the god what should be done.” He refuses to act on impulse, even when the impulse would be an act of mercy. This is the same Creon who deferred to Delphi at the start of the play, a man who trusts in divine guidance over his own judgment. The critic Bernard Knox, while acknowledging Creon’s reasonableness, also notes the irony that Creon, who in this play argues so eloquently against the attractions of kingship, will in Antigone cling to power with a deadly rigidity. Within the confines of Oedipus Rex, however, Creon’s character functions as a demonstration that Sophoclean wisdom often resides in the secondary character, the man who does not seek the spotlight. Creon embodies the Delphic maxim “nothing in excess.” He is not without ambition, but his ambition is checked by caution. He is not without pride, but his pride is subordinate to piety. He is, in the words of the scholar R. P. Winnington-Ingram, “the man who survives because he does not demand to know everything, who accepts the limits of human knowledge and power.” Thus, Creon’s character is essential to the moral economy of the play. He provides a norm against which Oedipus’s excesses can be measured. Where Oedipus leaps to conclusions, Creon waits for evidence. Where Oedipus trusts his own intelligence absolutely, Creon consults the gods. Where Oedipus lashes out, Creon negotiates. This is not to say that Creon is the hero of the play; he is not. His very moderation precludes the kind of grandeur that makes Oedipus a tragic figure. But he is the steady ground against which the earthquake of

Oedipus’s fall can be felt, and his assumption of power at the end restores a sense of order, however fragile, to a city shaken by revelation. 4. Comment on the Role of the Chorus in Oedipus the King (First and Second Speech) The Chorus of Oedipus Rex, composed of fifteen Theban elders, is far more than a lyrical interlude between episodes. It is a character with a collective consciousness, a moral barometer, and an active participant in the unfolding tragedy. Its odes — the first speech (the Parodos) and the second speech (the First Stasimon) — are especially crucial, for they establish the religious and emotional atmosphere of the play, articulate the fears and hopes of the citizen body, and respond to each stage of the revelation with a deepening sense of dread. To examine these two speeches closely is to see how Sophocles uses the Chorus to bridge the world of the audience and the world of the characters, and to create a communal voice that amplifies the tragedy’s themes of piety, pollution, and the fragility of human greatness. The Parodos (lines 151–215) opens the choral song immediately after the prologue. Oedipus has announced his determination to find Laius’s murderer, and the people have been summoned. The Chorus enters, and its first speech is a prayer to the gods. The lyric is divided into strophes and antistrophes, and it moves with an intense, terrified energy. The opening lines invoke a catalogue of Olympian powers: “Sweet-voiced daughter of Zeus, Athena, hear us; and Artemis, our protector, and Phoebus Apollo, lord of arrows.” The elders are in a state of panic, and their prayer is not a formulaic ritual but a desperate cry for deliverance from the plague that is destroying the city. The imagery is graphic: “The city is dying, the crops of the land wither, the herds of cattle are barren, women bring forth stillborn children, and the plague, that fiery god, has fallen on Thebes and drives the city to ruin.” The Chorus personifies the plague as a living, malevolent deity, and in doing so it gives voice to the raw terror of a pre-scientific community that experiences catastrophe as divine wrath. The critic R. W. B. Burton, in The Chorus in Sophocles’ Tragedies, notes that the Parodos functions to “establish the cosmic framework in which the events of the play are to be understood.” The plague is not merely a backdrop; it is a sign that the natural and divine orders have been broken, and the Chorus’s opening song places the entire drama under the sign of ritual crisis. The Parodos also introduces one of the play’s key ironic motifs: the appeal to the gods who will, by the end of the play, be revealed as the ultimate authors of the disaster. The Chorus prays to Apollo specifically: “Phoebus Apollo, lord of healing, send us your aid.” Yet Apollo is the god who sent the plague, who sent the oracle, who set the trap in which Oedipus is already caught. The prayer for healing is a prayer to the very power that requires, as the price of healing, the unmasking of the king. The audience, familiar with the myth, hears in the Chorus’s supplication a tragic irony: the god will heal Thebes, but only by destroying its ruler. The Chorus’s innocent piety thus becomes an unwitting element of the tragic machinery. Structurally, the Parodos moves from lamentation to a more specific plea that Zeus and Apollo turn their weapons against the invisible enemy: “Father Zeus, let the murderer, whoever he is, be destroyed by your thunderbolt.” The elders imagine the killer as an external threat, a polluted man hiding in the wilds. The irony deepens: the killer is standing right before them, speaking the prologue. The Chorus, the voice of the city, cannot see what the audience already knows. This gap between choral knowledge and audience knowledge is one of the most powerful devices of Greek tragedy. It makes the Chorus a figure of collective blindness, a well-meaning community that loves its king and cannot imagine the truth that is about to be revealed. This blindness will be systematically stripped away over the course of the play. The First Stasimon (lines 463–512), the second speech, follows the confrontation between Oedipus and Teiresias. The prophet has just accused Oedipus of being the murderer and has spoken darkly of incest and blindness. The Chorus is shaken, confused, and torn between its loyalty to Oedipus and its fear of the prophetic voice. The ode begins with a remarkable image of the unknown murderer as a hunted fugitive: “Who is the man of whom the divine Delphic rock spoke, the man whose hands have done a deed of unspeakable horror? Let him flee, faster than the storm-swift horses, for the son of Zeus, Apollo, leaps upon him with fire and lightning.” The lyric is full of movement and terror, painting the picture of a man pursued by divine vengeance. The irony, again, is that the man they imagine fleeing into the wild is the king they have just been addressing. The Chorus

does not yet connect the description to Oedipus, but the audience does, and the gap between their vision and our own creates the unbearable tension that is the hallmark of Sophoclean suspense. Then the Chorus pivots, and its loyalty to Oedipus reasserts itself. “Yet I am troubled,” the elders sing. “How could I ever agree with those who speak against the king? I saw with my own eyes how he came to this city and saved us from the Sphinx. He proved himself wise and beloved of heaven.” This is a crucial moment for the characterisation of the Chorus. They are not neutral observers; they are Oedipus’s subjects and, in a real sense, his friends. They have a stake in his innocence. Their memory of the Sphinx episode is a touchstone of civic gratitude. The critic Charles Segal, in Oedipus Tyrannus: Tragic Heroism and the Limits of Knowledge, argues that this oscillation between fear of the oracle’s truth and loyalty to the king is the central dramatic function of the Chorus. They dramatise the process by which a community clings to its faith in a leader even as evidence against him mounts. The Chorus’s reluctance to believe Teiresias is not stupidity; it is human, understandable, and it makes the eventual collapse of that faith all the more devastating. The stasimon ends with a declaration that only the gods can know the truth: “Zeus and Apollo know the affairs of men and are wise; but that a mortal seer knows more than I — there is no true test of that. A man may have wisdom, but I will never accept the accusations against the king until I see them proven.” This is a rational, cautious position, and it aligns the Chorus with Creon’s moderate, evidence-based approach. Yet it also reveals the limits of human reason. The elders demand proof, and proof will be provided — the testimony of the Shepherds, the identity of the foundling — but the proof will destroy the king they seek to protect. The First Stasimon thus encapsulates the tragedy’s central epistemological crisis: the conflict between the desire for certainty and the horror of what certainty brings. Beyond these two speeches, the Chorus’s broader role is to provide a barometer of the emotional temperature. Its language shifts from terrified prayer, to perplexed doubt, to horrified recognition, and finally to a profound, sorrowful meditation on the fragility of human happiness. The final ode — “What man, what man on earth, wins more of happiness than a seeming, and after that a turning away?” — is one of the great existential statements in Greek literature. But it is in the Parodos and the First Stasimon that the Chorus most actively shapes the dramatic rhythm, moving the audience from collective anxiety to the first tremors of suspicion, and binding the individual tragedy of Oedipus to the larger fate of the polis. 5. Analyze the Character of Oedipus as a Tragic Hero as Delineated by Sophocles in Oedipus the King Oedipus is, for Aristotle and for the Western critical tradition he initiated, the tragic hero par excellence. In the Poetics, Aristotle uses Oedipus Rex as his primary example to illustrate the qualities that produce the finest tragic effect: a protagonist who is neither perfectly good nor utterly wicked, whose fall from prosperity to misery is caused not by vice but by a great hamartia (an error or frailty), and whose story evokes pity and fear precisely because we recognise in him something of our own capacity for greatness and blindness. Sophocles’ Oedipus is a man of immense intellectual power, moral courage, and passionate commitment to truth, and these very virtues, operating in a world governed by a hidden divine pattern, become the instruments of his annihilation. To analyse him as a tragic hero is to examine the internal logic by which his strengths become his weaknesses, his knowledge becomes his ignorance, and his freedom becomes his doom. The first quality of the Sophoclean tragic hero is his extraordinary stature. Oedipus is introduced as the saviour of Thebes, the man who solved the Sphinx’s riddle and freed the city from the monster’s oppression. The priest in the prologue addresses him in language that borders on the divine: “You are not equal to the gods, we know; but we have come to you as the first of men, both in the ordinary crises of life and in the dealings of men with the gods.” Oedipus accepts this role with a confidence that is magnificent and, in retrospect, deeply pitiable. He declares that his heart “groans for the city, for the people, and for myself,” and that he has already sent Creon to Delphi before the people even asked. He is a king who identifies his own well-being entirely with the well-being of his subjects. His opening speech is full of compassion, energy, and a sense of personal responsibility that is precisely what one would wish for in a leader. The critic Bernard Knox, in The Heroic Temper, shows that this immense self-confidence is the core of Sophoclean heroism. The tragic hero refuses to compromise, refuses to yield, refuses to accept limits. He pushes through every obstacle with a terrifying singleness of purpose. That is his greatness, and that is also his destruction.

The key to the tragic structure is Aristotle’s concept of hamartia, often mistranslated as “tragic flaw” but better understood as an error in judgment or a blind spot that leads to the fall. Oedipus’s hamartia is not a single moral failing but a complex of traits — intellectual pride, rashness of temper, and an overweening trust in his own capacity to uncover and control the truth — that cause him to commit the fatal mistake of pressing the investigation to its bitter end. Every step of the inquiry that destroys him is taken voluntarily, indeed driven by his own insistence. The prophet Teiresias tells him the truth, and Oedipus dismisses it. Jocasta begs him to stop, and he refuses. The Shepherd begs him not to ask, and he threatens the old man with torture. Oedipus’s relentless pursuit of the truth is not compelled by any external force; it is the pure expression of his character. As E. R. Dodds argues, “Oedipus is destroyed not because he defies the gods or commits a crime in a state of moral wickedness, but because he is the kind of man who, in the very act of trying to avoid his fate, rushes headlong into it.” This brings us to the tragic irony that defines his journey. Oedipus is the master interpreter of riddles, yet he cannot interpret the riddle of his own life. He is the man with the clearest eyes, yet he is blind. He condemns the murderer of Laius with curses that fall upon his own head. Every speech he makes is laden with a double meaning that the audience perceives and he does not. When he declares, “I will fight for Laius as if he were my own father,” the words are literally true in a way he cannot imagine. This irony is not merely a stylistic device; it is the very substance of his tragic situation. The scholar Jean-Pierre Vernant, in Myth and Tragedy in Ancient Greece, describes Oedipus as “the man of ambiguities,” a figure who embodies the collision between human intelligence and divine foreknowledge. Oedipus’s tragedy is that he is the wrong man in the wrong place at the wrong time, but it is also that he is precisely the right man — the only man with the courage and intelligence — to uncover the truth and face it. The fall of Oedipus is marked by the classic tragic sequence of peripeteia and anagnorisis, as discussed earlier. The reversal turns the triumphant king into the polluted outcast; the recognition turns the confident knower into the man who cannot bear to see. His self-blinding is the most powerful symbolic act in Greek tragedy. Critics have debated whether the blinding is an act of despair or of heroic self-assertion. I would argue, following Knox, that it is the final, terrible expression of Oedipus’s heroic autonomy. He could not control his fate, but he will control the manner in which he meets it. He will not look upon the children who are also his siblings; he will not see the light that witnessed his crimes; he will impose upon his body the physical darkness that has been the condition of his soul. The self-blinding is simultaneously a punishment, a recognition, and a defiant gesture. As he says, “It was Apollo, friends, Apollo who brought these sufferings on me; but the hand that struck the blow was mine alone.” This line is the crux of the play’s theology and psychology. Divine fate and human responsibility are held in a single sentence, not reconciled but placed side by side in a paradox that is the essence of the tragic. Oedipus’s final speeches, after the blinding, mark his emergence as a figure of immense suffering and purified grandeur. He begs for exile, embraces his daughters, and accepts the justice of his own curses. The Chorus, which had once revered him as the saviour, now sees him as the supreme example of human fragility: “Look, all you who dwell in Thebes, look on Oedipus. Here is the man who solved the famous riddle, who was a man of surpassing power. Who among the citizens did not gaze on his success with envy? But now, into what a fearful sea of troubles he has fallen.” This choral envoi is the definitive statement of the tragic hero’s function: to teach, through the spectacle of his suffering, the limits of human existence. As Aristotle wrote, the tragic hero’s story “by means of pity and fear brings about the catharsis of such emotions.” We pity Oedipus because his suffering exceeds anything he deserved; we fear him because his fate is the extreme instance of a vulnerability that belongs to all mortals. In sum, Sophocles’ Oedipus is the tragic hero because he unites in a single figure the highest human excellence and the deepest human misery. His intelligence, courage, and devotion to his city are real and admirable; they are not masks concealing a secret villainy. And it is precisely these admirable qualities, placed in the crucible of a world governed by oracles and hidden pasts, that shatter him. He falls, but in falling he becomes, as the blinded Lear on the heath or the broken Antony at the base of the monument, a larger soul than he was in prosperity. He is the tragic hero not because he is good, nor because he is flawed, but because he is great — and greatness, in the Sophoclean cosmos, is the most dangerous gift the gods can bestow.
