## Feminist Literary Criticism: Theory and Practice

Introduction
Feminist literary criticism emerged as a distinct approach in the 1960s and 1970s, challenging the male-dominated canon and examining how literature reflects and reinforces gender ideologies.

Key Figures
Elaine Showalter's concept of "gynocriticism" focuses on women as writers and the female literary tradition. Sandra Gilbert and Susan Gubar's "The Madwoman in the Attic" examines representations of women in nineteenth-century literature.

Major Concepts
The "male gaze" theory, developed from Laura Mulvey's work in film theory, examines how visual arts and literature position women as objects of male scrutiny. Intersectionality, coined by Kimberle Crenshaw, explores how gender intersects with race, class, and other identities.

Applications
Applying feminist criticism to works like Jane Austen's "Pride and Prejudice" reveals how the marriage plot both reinforces and subverts patriarchal norms. Virginia Woolf's "A Room of One's Own" remains a foundational text for feminist literary analysis.

Conclusion
Feminist criticism continues to evolve, incorporating queer theory, postcolonial perspectives, and digital humanities approaches to literary analysis.
