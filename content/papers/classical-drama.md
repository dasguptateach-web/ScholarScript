## The Influence of Classical Drama on Elizabethan Theatre

## Abstract This paper examines the profound influence of classical Greek and Roman drama on the development of Elizabethan theatre. The University Wits, particularly Christopher Marlowe and Thomas Kyd, synthesized classical forms with native English traditions to create the romantic drama that reached its apex in Shakespeare's works.

## Introduction The Elizabethan age stands as the most glorious period in English dramatic history. However, this achievement did not emerge from a vacuum. The drama of this period represents a sophisticated synthesis of classical traditions inherited from Greece and Rome, medieval religious drama, and native English folk performances.

## Classical Influences The Roman dramatist Seneca provided the model for Elizabethan tragedy. His plays featured five-act structure, rhetorical speeches, ghosts, and revenge themes. The first English tragedy, "Gorboduc" (1561) by Sackville and Norton, explicitly followed the Senecan model, complete with chorus and messenger speeches.

## Conclusion The Elizabethan dramatists succeeded where their predecessors had failed because they were able to combine classical form with native English vigor. They took what was useful from the ancient world and forged a new, distinctly English dramatic tradition.
