May 16, 2026 [Module IV: STRUCTURALISM]

Saussure criticism because texts began to be understood as systems of signs producing meaning through convention and difference. Ferdinand de Saussure is regarded as the father of Structuralism because he revolutionized the study of language by treating it as a system of relations rather than a mere collection of words. In his influential work Course in General Linguistics, he argued that meaning arises through difference. A word means what it does because it is different from other words within the linguistic system. Saussure famously remarked, "In language there are only differences without positive terms." This insight deeply influenced twentieth-century literary criticism because critics began to see literature not as isolated artistic expression but as part of larger cultural and linguistic structures. Structuralists therefore searched for recurring patterns, oppositions, and codes underlying literary texts. Saussure's theories can be clearly applied to literary analysis. In Hamlet, the meaning of Hamlet's character emerges through a network of oppositions: action versus hesitation, appearance versus reality, sanity versus madness. Hamlet's famous statement, "Seems, madam? Nay, it is; I know not 'seems'," reflects the instability between external signs and inner truth. Structuralist critics argue that these binary oppositions generate the play's meaning. Similarly, in Pride and Prejudice, the contrast between reason and emotion, pride and humility, structures the narrative. Saussure's influence also appears in the work of Claude Lévi-Strauss, who analysed myths as systems of recurring oppositions such as life/death or nature/culture. Thus literature becomes understandable through its hidden structures rather than solely through authorial intention.

Literary works often depend upon signs that carry symbolic and cultural meanings. In The Great Gatsby, the green light at Daisy's dock functions as a powerful sign. On the surface it is merely a physical light, but symbolically it signifies Gatsby's impossible dream, desire, and the illusion of the American Dream itself. Gatsby's constant reaching toward the light transforms it into a cultural sign of unattainable aspiration. Similarly, in Heart of Darkness, darkness becomes a recurring sign associated with colonial greed, moral corruption, and psychological terror. Structuralist critics argue that these signs gain meaning not independently but through their relation to opposing signs such as light/civilization or hope/despair. Roland Barthes extended this theory in Mythologies by showing how ordinary objects and images function as cultural signs loaded with ideological meanings. Poststructuralist thinkers later questioned whether signs can ever produce stable meanings. Jacques Derrida argued that every sign refers endlessly to other signs, creating instability. For example, in Waiting for Godot, the mysterious figure of Godot functions as an uncertain sign whose meaning remains unresolved. Critics have interpreted him as God, hope, salvation, or simply meaninglessness itself. This ambiguity demonstrates how signs resist fixed interpretation. Nevertheless, the Structuralist idea of signs remains foundational because it enables readers to analyse how literature and culture communicate meaning through symbols, patterns, and systems of representation.

Signifier and Signified

Critics have repeatedly emphasized Saussure's lasting importance. Jonathan Culler observed that Saussure made language "the model for all systems of meaning." However, later Poststructuralists questioned his belief in stable structures. Jacques Derrida argued that meaning constantly slips from one sign to another and can never be fully fixed. Yet even Derrida's critique depended upon Saussure's linguistic foundation. Literary criticism today continues to use Saussurean ideas when analysing symbolism, narrative patterns, and cultural codes. His theories transformed literature into a field where language itself became the central object of inquiry.

The distinction between "signifier" and "signified" was introduced by Ferdinand de Saussure in Course in General Linguistics and became one of the central principles of Structuralism. The signifier refers to the sound-image or written form of a word, while the signified refers to the mental concept associated with it. Saussure emphasized that the relationship between the two is arbitrary. The word "king" has no natural connection with the idea of monarchy; society simply agrees upon the relation. He compared signifier and signified to "two sides of a sheet of paper." This theory transformed literary criticism because critics began examining how words and symbols create layers of meaning rather than signs directly reflecting reality.

The concept of the "sign" is one of the most influential ideas in Structuralism and originates from the linguistic theories of Ferdinand de Saussure. In Course in General Linguistics, Saussure explained that language is made up of signs, each consisting of a signifier (sound or written form) and a signified (mental concept). The relation between them is arbitrary. There is no natural reason why the word "rose" should refer to a flower. Saussure stated, "The bond between the signifier and the signified is arbitrary." This insight changed literary criticism because texts began to be understood as systems of signs producing meaning through convention and difference.

Literary texts provide numerous examples of the relationship between signifier and signified. In Macbeth, blood functions as a recurring signifier. At first it signifies bravery and honour in battle, but later it becomes associated with guilt, violence, and psychological torment. Lady Macbeth's cry, "Out, damned spot!" reveals how the signifier "blood" carries shifting meanings throughout the play. Similarly, in The Scarlet Letter, the scarlet "A" initially signifies adultery and shame, but gradually comes to signify strength, resilience, and even sanctity in the eyes of society. Structuralist critics show that literary meaning emerges from such evolving relationships between signifiers and signified concepts within the text's structure.

Poststructuralists challenged the stability of this relationship. Jacques Derrida argued that the signified itself becomes another signifier in an endless chain of meanings. Thus language can never provide complete certainty. This instability is visible in The Waste Land, where fragmented images and symbols continually shift meaning. Water, for example, may signify death, rebirth, purification, or spiritual emptiness depending on context. Critics such as Terry Eagleton note that Derrida transformed Saussure's linguistic theory into a philosophy of uncertainty. Yet the concepts of signifier and signified remain essential tools for analysing symbolism, metaphor, and representation in literature.

Langue and Parole

The concepts of "langue" and "parole" were introduced by Ferdinand de Saussure in Course in General Linguistics. Langue refers to the collective system of language — its grammar, rules, and conventions shared by society — whereas parole refers to individual acts of speech or writing. Saussure believed that language should be studied scientifically through langue because it represents the underlying structure enabling communication. He observed, "Language is not a function of the speaker; it is a product passively assimilated by the individual." Structuralists later applied this distinction to literature, arguing that individual texts are examples of parole shaped by larger systems or conventions functioning as langue.

Literary genres clearly illustrate this theory. Shakespeare's Romeo and Juliet is an individual example of parole, but it follows the broader conventions of tragic drama, which constitute langue. Elements such as doomed lovers, family conflict, and catastrophic endings belong to the structural system of tragedy inherited from earlier literary traditions. Likewise, detective novels such as The Hound of the Baskervilles follow recognizable structural patterns — mystery, clues, investigation, and revelation. Structuralist critics analyse these conventions as the "grammar" of literature. Claude Lévi-Strauss similarly studied myths from different cultures and discovered recurring narrative structures beneath individual stories.

Poststructuralists later criticized the rigid separation between langue and parole. Jacques Derrida argued that structures themselves are unstable and subject to endless reinterpretation. Literary texts frequently subvert the conventions they inherit. For instance, Waiting for Godot deliberately breaks the conventional structure of dramatic action. Traditional expectations of plot and resolution collapse into repetition and uncertainty. Thus the relationship between system and expression becomes unstable. Critics such as Roland Barthes further destabilized Structuralism through his theory of "the death of the author," which rejects fixed authorial meaning.

Even the distinction between langue and parole eventually shifted attention from stable literary systems to the plurality of interpretation. Nevertheless, the distinction remains valuable for understanding how individual literary works emerge from broader cultural and linguistic conventions.

Poststructuralism

Poststructuralism emerged during the 1960s as a reaction against the certainty and system-centred approach of Structuralism. While Structuralists believed that meaning could be discovered through stable linguistic structures, Poststructuralists argued that meaning is unstable and endlessly shifting. The movement is associated with thinkers such as Jacques Derrida, Michel Foucault, and Roland Barthes. Derrida's theory of deconstruction challenged the idea of fixed meanings and stable oppositions. His famous statement, "There is nothing outside the text," suggests that meaning is always mediated through language and interpretation. Poststructuralism therefore rejects the Structuralist dream of discovering permanent systems underlying literature and culture.

Literary texts often illustrate the instability emphasized by Poststructuralists. In Waiting for Godot, meaning constantly escapes certainty. Readers never learn who Godot is or whether he will arrive. The play resists closure and frustrates traditional interpretation. Similarly, The Waste Land presents fragmented voices, shifting symbols, and disconnected cultural references that undermine coherent meaning. Roland Barthes in The Death of the Author argued that the author no longer controls interpretation. Meaning depends upon readers and their cultural contexts. This idea transformed literary criticism because texts came to be seen as open fields of interpretation rather than closed systems with fixed meanings.

Critics have both praised and criticized Poststructuralism. Terry Eagleton remarked that Poststructuralism made readers "suspicious of all claims to absolute truth." It encouraged freedom of interpretation and exposed hidden ideologies within texts. However, opponents argue that excessive relativism can make interpretation chaotic. In novels like Midnight's Children, shifting narratives and unreliable memories demonstrate the Poststructuralist idea that truth itself is fragmented and unstable. Despite criticism, Poststructuralism profoundly changed literary theory by emphasizing ambiguity, multiplicity, and the endless play of language.

Difference between Structuralism and Poststructuralism

Structuralism and Poststructuralism are closely connected yet fundamentally opposed approaches to language and literature. Structuralism, inspired by Ferdinand de Saussure, believes that human culture operates through stable systems and structures. Meaning is produced through relations and oppositions within language. Structuralist critics therefore search for universal patterns governing myths, narratives, and literary texts. For example, in Oedipus Rex, Structuralists identify binary oppositions such as fate/free will, knowledge/ignorance, and blindness/vision. These oppositions organize the play's meaning. Structuralism thus treats literature as a structured system that can be scientifically analysed.

Poststructuralism challenges these assumptions by arguing that meaning is unstable and cannot be fixed within any structure. Jacques Derrida showed that binary oppositions are never stable because each term depends upon and undermines the other. In Hamlet, for example, the opposition between sanity and madness becomes blurred. Hamlet may be pretending to be mad, yet his behaviour also reveals genuine psychological instability. Similarly, in Frankenstein, distinctions between creator and monster, humanity and monstrosity, collapse under closer analysis. While Structuralists seek coherence, Poststructuralists emphasize ambiguity and contradiction.

Critics often describe Poststructuralism as both an extension and critique of Structuralism. Terry Eagleton notes that Poststructuralism emerged from "the cracks within Structuralism itself." Structuralism aims to uncover universal systems, whereas Poststructuralism questions whether any universal truth exists at all. In literature, Structuralists interpret texts as organized wholes, while Poststructuralists highlight fragmentation and interpretative instability. Modernist works like Ulysses and Postmodern texts like If on a winter's night a traveler especially reveal this shift from order to uncertainty. Together, these movements revolutionized literary criticism by transforming language itself into the central focus of interpretation.
