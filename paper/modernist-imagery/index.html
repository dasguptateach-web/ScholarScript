﻿This is a sample scholarly paper about the role of imagery in modernist poetry.

Modernist poets like T.S. Eliot and Ezra Pound revolutionised the use of imagery in poetry. Unlike their Romantic predecessors, who often used nature imagery to express personal emotion, the modernists adopted a more detached, objective approach.

The famous dictum "Show, don't tell" became central to modernist poetics. Ezra Pound defined the image as "that which presents an intellectual and emotional complex in an instant of time." This emphasis on precise, concrete imagery marked a decisive break from Victorian verbosity.

T.S. Eliot's "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock" exemplifies this new approach. The poem opens with a striking image: "Let us go then, you and I, / When the evening is spread out against the sky / Like a patient etherised upon a table." This clinical, unsettling simile sets the tone for the entire poem and reflects the modernist fascination with urban alienation.

In conclusion, the modernist revolution in poetry was fundamentally a revolution in imagery. By prioritising precise, objective images over abstract statement, poets like Eliot and Pound created a new poetic language capable of expressing the fragmented consciousness of the modern world.
