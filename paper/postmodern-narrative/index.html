Fractured Selves: Narrative Identity in Postmodern Fiction

Introduction

Postmodern fiction challenges the notion of a unified, coherent self. Through fragmentation, metafiction, and narrative instability, writers like Thomas Pynchon and Don DeLillo explore identity as a construct rather than an essence.

Pynchon and Paranoia as Structure

In The Crying of Lot 49, Thomas Pynchon uses the conspiracy plot as a structural analogue for the fragmented postmodern subject. Oedipa Maas's journey through the labyrinthine systems of meaning reflects the impossibility of a stable identity.

DeLillo and the Mediated Self

Don DeLillo's White Noise examines how media saturation creates a fractured sense of self. The characters define themselves through brand names, television images, and academic jargon.

Conclusion

Postmodern narrative techniques do more than experiment with form; they enact the very fragmentation they describe, making the reader experience the instability of identity.
